# Foreword

Goalkeeping is the most technical position on a football pitch. It is also the most poorly documented. A defender knows what a Cruyff turn is. A midfielder can name a half-volley. But ask three goalkeepers what a K-barrier looks like and you will get three different answers, two of which are wrong. This is not the goalkeepers' fault. The vocabulary has been passed down by demonstration — coach to coach, dressing room to dressing room — and a lot of it has been lost in translation between Italian, German, Dutch, Spanish, English, and now Portuguese-Brazilian, where modern goalkeeping has gone a long way in the last fifteen years.

So this is an attempt to fix that. Every technique that a working professional goalkeeper coach should be able to name, demonstrate, and correct on the training pitch — listed, defined, and broken down to the level of detail you need when a young keeper is doing it wrong and you must explain to them why.

I have organised the encyclopedia by family. We start with what every save begins from — the set position, footwork, the architecture of the hands. Then we move outward: catching, parrying, diving, aerial work, one-against-one, sweeper play, penalties, reactive saves, and finally the conditions that change everything — wet pitches, wind, sun. At the end you will find a glossary, a quick-reference card of coaching cues, and a list of the most common errors and how to correct them.

A few principles guide what is included and what is not. First, I have privileged the names that working professional coaches actually use, even when those names are inelegant. The K-barrier is called a K-barrier because it looks like the letter K — and that is what coaches across the continent have been calling it since it migrated from futsal in the early 2000s. I do not invent prettier names; the goalkeepers and their coaches have already chosen.

Second, I have tried to be honest about disagreements. The Italian school, the Dutch school, the German school, and the modern Spanish-Portuguese school do not always agree on the right answer to a given problem. Where there is genuine disagreement among the best coaches in the world, I say so. Where one method has been clearly superseded by another, I say that too.

Third, this is a reference, not a textbook. You are not meant to read it cover to cover. You are meant to look up a technique on Friday evening because you want to coach it correctly on Saturday morning, or because you are reviewing match video on Sunday and need the precise word for what your keeper did wrong on the goal in the 73rd minute. Use it that way.

There is no greater pleasure in a coaching life than seeing a goalkeeper, after years of patient work, make the save that wins their team the league. The save will look easy. It will look like luck. It will be neither. It will be the product of a thousand small decisions, made correctly, in the right sequence, against the clock — most of them invisible to anyone watching. This book is about those invisible thousand.

Read it. Argue with it. Take what is useful and leave what is not. And then go and coach your goalkeeper.

*— A coach's note, written for the next generation.*

# How To Use This Reference

Each technique entry is organised in the same way, so you can flip to any page and find what you need in the same place every time.

#### The header gives you the technique.

The first line of every entry is the proper name of the technique, followed in parentheses by the most common variants used in different European traditions. Where a technique has migrated from another sport — futsal, handball, ice hockey — that origin is noted, because it usually explains why the movement looks the way it does.

#### The summary box tells you when to use it.

Below the header, a small summary table gives you four pieces of information at a glance: when this technique is the right answer, what its primary alternatives are, what part of the body initiates the movement, and what the most common failure mode is. This is what you check before you coach the technique, to make sure you are coaching the right one for the situation.

#### The mechanics section breaks down the movement.

This is the longest section of every entry. It describes the movement in the order the body performs it: preparation, initiation, execution, recovery. The level of detail is deliberately high. You should be able to read the mechanics section, watch the technique on video, and identify exactly where the movement breaks down for an individual goalkeeper.

#### Coaching cues are short and concrete.

These are the words you say on the training pitch. They are not explanations — they are triggers. A goalkeeper in motion does not have time to process a paragraph; they need a word or a phrase that snaps the body into the right shape. The cues here are the ones I have found work most reliably across goalkeepers of different ages and languages.

#### Common errors are the ones you will actually see.

Not the errors a beginner makes for one week and then never again, but the persistent errors that even professionals fall back into when they are tired, under pressure, or have stopped paying attention to the small things. These are what you watch for in match video.

#### A note on side preference.

All descriptions assume a right-handed goalkeeper. For a left-handed keeper, mirror the technique. Where a technique has a meaningfully different feel on the strong side versus the weak side — and many do — I note it explicitly.

# Part I — First Principles

*Before any save technique can be discussed, the foundation must be in place. Every save in this encyclopedia begins from one of a small number of body shapes, executed through one of a small number of footwork patterns, with the hands held in one of a small number of architectures. If the foundation is wrong, the technique that follows cannot be right — and no amount of athletic talent will paper over the gap.*

*The four chapters in this part — the set position, footwork, body shape and weight, and the architecture of the hands — describe the building blocks. Read them first. Return to them often. The most common mistake in coaching young goalkeepers is to teach the spectacular saves before the foundations are automatic. The most common mistake in coaching senior goalkeepers is to assume the foundations are still automatic when in fact they have decayed.*

## Chapter 1 — The Set Position

The set position is the body shape a goalkeeper adopts in the moment immediately before the shot is struck. It is not a stance you stand in. It is a stance you arrive at, hold for a fraction of a second, and explode out of. Every save in this book begins from a version of it. If you teach a goalkeeper one thing, teach the set.

### 1.1 — The Standard Set

**Use when**

A shot is imminent and the goalkeeper has time to prepare.

**Alternatives**

Low set (close-range), high set (long-range), set-set (1v1).

**Initiator**

The feet stop moving; the weight settles forward over the toes.

**Most common failure**

Caught flat-footed on the heels — the goalkeeper is in the position but not on it.

#### Mechanics

Feet are shoulder-width apart, parallel, with the studs of the front of the boot — not the heels — in contact with the grass. The weight is forward enough that if the goalkeeper's heels were lifted by a millimetre, they would not fall backwards. The knees are flexed to roughly 130–140 degrees — soft enough to drive in any direction, firm enough to stand on. The hips are hinged forward so the line of the spine runs at a slight angle toward the ball. The chest is square to the line of the shot, not to the ball — these are different things if the ball is moving.

The hands are held at roughly waist height, palms facing each other, a ball-width apart. The elbows are slightly flexed and held just outside the rib cage, not pinned to the body and not extended. The head is up and the eyes are on the ball. The classic Dutch coaching cue — neus over de tenen, nose over the toes — captures the entire upper-body shape in three words.

The set is held for the smallest possible amount of time. The keeper arrives at the set as the striker plants the standing foot. The keeper explodes from the set as the striker's contact foot makes contact with the ball. If the set is held longer than this, the keeper has arrived early and is wasting time on the heels. If the keeper is still moving as contact is made, they are not set, and they will not save the shot.

**Coaching cue.  **“Stop, set, save.” Three words, three actions, in that order. Stop the feet. Set the body. Save the ball. The keeper who tries to combine the first two into a single fluid movement will save the easy shots and miss the hard ones.

#### Common errors

The most common error at every level — youth, semi-professional, and Champions League — is heel contact. The keeper looks set on video, but the weight is sitting one centimetre too far back. From this position, the first movement of any save is a tiny correction forward, and that correction takes time the keeper does not have. The fix is not technical, it is attentional. Have the keeper hold the set with their heels lifted off the ground by a paper's thickness. Drill it until the muscle memory is unmistakeable.

The second-most-common error is over-flexion of the knees. A keeper who sets too low loses lateral range — the legs cannot drive sideways from a deep squat as efficiently as from a moderate flex. The set is not a wrestling stance. The cue is firm legs, soft knees.

The third error is asymmetry. The keeper drifts toward their dominant side without realising it, so the strong-side dive is shorter and faster while the weak-side dive is longer and slower. Watch for this in video. It is invisible from behind the goal and obvious from above.

### 1.2 — The Low Set (Close Range)

**Use when**

The shot is from inside 12 metres or the keeper has rushed forward to close angle.

**Alternatives**

Standard set (mid-range), set-set (1v1).

**Initiator**

The hips drop while the chest stays tall.

**Most common failure**

Dropping the head with the hips — eyes leave the ball.

#### Mechanics

The low set is the same architecture as the standard set, with two adjustments. The hips drop another fifteen to twenty centimetres, lowering the centre of mass closer to the ground where most close-range shots will travel. The hands drop with the hips, settling at roughly mid-thigh height with the palms still facing forward. The chest remains tall — this is the critical detail. A goalkeeper who collapses the chest forward as the hips drop has lost the ability to react to a shot above the waist; they are committed to going down and only down.

This shape is what the great Dutch and Italian coaches mean when they talk about being big and small at the same time. Big in the upper body — the chest, shoulders, and arms occupy as much horizontal space as the keeper can manage. Small in the base — the centre of gravity is low, ready to explode in any direction without first dropping. A keeper who is only big looks impressive but cannot get down to a low shot. A keeper who is only small looks compact but cannot stretch to a shot at the top corner.

**Coaching cue.  **“Hips down, eyes up.” The lower body drops, the upper body holds. If you cannot see the goalkeeper's eyes from in front, the chest has collapsed and the set has failed.

### 1.3 — The Set-Set (1v1 Set)

**Use when**

The attacker is dribbling toward the keeper with the ball at their feet.

**Alternatives**

Smother (if the attacker has taken a heavy touch), K-barrier (final stage of the closeout).

**Initiator**

Each touch the attacker takes — the keeper sets, holds, and resets in rhythm with the touches.

**Most common failure**

Setting once and staying set — the keeper becomes a stationary target.

#### Mechanics

The set-set is not a single shape but a rhythm. The attacker dribbles forward; the keeper advances in small fast steps, getting set on each of the attacker's touches and exploding forward again the moment the ball leaves the attacker's foot. The keeper is in the set position only when the attacker is in contact with the ball — which is the only moment a shot can be taken. Between touches, the keeper is moving, closing space, and shrinking the angle.

This rhythm is what FIFA technical analysts have called explosiveness from the set. It is the single biggest visible change in elite goalkeeping over the last fifteen years. A goalkeeper who set once at the edge of the area and waited for the shot has been replaced by a goalkeeper who sets, advances, sets again, advances, and sets a third time before deciding whether to save the ball or block it. Each set is shorter than the one before. The final set, immediately before the shot, may last only a tenth of a second.

The hand position in the set-set is slightly wider than in the standard set — closer to the K-barrier shape we will describe in Chapter 32. This is a deliberate compromise: the keeper is preparing for either a saved shot or a blocked attempt, and the wider hands give them a head start on the block if the shot does not come.

**Coaching cue.  **“Match their feet.” The keeper's feet stop and start in rhythm with the attacker's touches. If the keeper's feet are still moving when the attacker shoots, the keeper has lost. If the keeper has been still too long, the angle has closed but the keeper is now flat and reactive rather than dynamic.

## Chapter 2 — Footwork

The hands save the ball; the feet put the hands in the right place. This is the oldest line in goalkeeper coaching and it is still the truest. A keeper with poor hands and great feet will become a competent goalkeeper. A keeper with great hands and poor feet will not. Train the feet first.

### 2.1 — The Side-Step (Shuffle)

**Use when**

Adjusting position by less than a metre, or tracking a shifting angle.

**Alternatives**

Cross-step (longer distances), drop-step (backwards diagonal).

**Initiator**

The leading foot moves first, the trailing foot follows but does not cross.

**Most common failure**

Crossing the feet under pressure — instant loss of balance.

#### Mechanics

The side-step is the keeper's default lateral movement. The lead foot moves toward the target, the trail foot follows it, and the feet never cross. The body remains square to the ball throughout. The centre of gravity stays at a constant height — the keeper does not bob up and down. The pace of the step is matched to the speed of the ball: small fast shuffles for a moving ball, larger steps for a stationary one.

The hands are held in the standard set throughout. This is critical. A keeper who drops their hands while shuffling is not in a set position when the shot comes — they are between positions, and they will be late.

The Italian coaching tradition emphasises a particular rhythm — passo e mezzo, a step and a half — meaning the keeper takes one full lateral step and finds themselves halfway through the next one when they need to set. This rhythm prevents both over-shuffling (taking too many small steps and arriving late) and under-shuffling (one big step and a stationary set, which loses adjustability).

### 2.2 — The Cross-Step

**Use when**

Covering more than two metres laterally, especially across the goal mouth.

**Alternatives**

Side-step (shorter distances), drop-step (changing depth and width simultaneously).

**Initiator**

The trail leg crosses in front of the lead leg, generating one long stride.

**Most common failure**

Cross-stepping when a side-step would suffice — body squareness is lost.

#### Mechanics

The cross-step covers more ground per unit of energy than the side-step but at the cost of body orientation. As the trail leg crosses in front of the lead leg, the hips rotate roughly thirty degrees away from square. For a brief moment the keeper's chest is no longer pointed at the ball. This is acceptable when the keeper is not yet in the firing line — for example, repositioning across the box after a passage of play has shifted from one side to the other. It is not acceptable when a shot is imminent.

The cross-step ends with a recovery step that returns the body to square, followed by a set. The keeper does not save from a cross-step; they save from the set that follows it.

**Coaching cue.  **“Cross to travel, shuffle to save.” The cross-step is for covering ground. The shuffle is for arriving in position. They are different tools and should be selected, not interchanged.

### 2.3 — The Drop-Step

**Use when**

The keeper must move backwards and laterally simultaneously — typically tracking a ball that has been chipped over their position.

**Alternatives**

Backpedal (purely backward movement), turn-and-run (when the chip is too deep to recover).

**Initiator**

The lead leg pivots ninety degrees and steps backward at a forty-five degree angle.

**Most common failure**

Backpedalling instead of drop-stepping — the keeper falls backwards with their feet under their body.

#### Mechanics

The drop-step is the single most important movement for a modern, high-line goalkeeper. As soon as the keeper recognises that the ball is going behind them, the lead leg — the leg on the side the ball is travelling — pivots open and steps backward and outward at a roughly forty-five degree angle. The opposite leg follows in a powerful crossing stride, generating speed and depth simultaneously. The body has now turned ninety degrees and the keeper is running in a straight line toward the point where the ball will land. The eyes never leave the ball; the head is turned over the leading shoulder.

This is fundamentally different from a backpedal, in which the keeper attempts to move backward while still facing forward. The backpedal is slow, balance-precarious, and exposes the keeper to falling onto their back. The drop-step generates immediate acceleration in the correct direction with the keeper still able to make a save at the end of it.

**Coaching cue.  **“Open the gate.” The lead leg swings open like a gate, hinging at the hip. Once the gate is open, the second leg drives through it. Keepers who drop-step well have a distinctive opening movement that the keeper coach can spot immediately.

### 2.4 — The Power Step

**Use when**

Generating maximum lateral or diagonal extension for a diving save.

**Alternatives**

Collapse (no lateral movement, the keeper drops in place).

**Initiator**

The lead leg drives at a forty-five degree angle toward the corner the ball is travelling to.

**Most common failure**

Stepping sideways rather than forward and sideways — the dive becomes a fall.

#### Mechanics

The power step is the launching mechanism for every diving save above ankle height. From the set position, the keeper drives the lead leg — the leg on the side of the ball — at a precisely forty-five degree angle toward the corner the ball is heading to. The forty-five degrees is not approximate; coaches measure this on video to the degree. The step covers approximately one shoe-length of ground; the explosive contact with the grass is what generates the force that carries the keeper into the air.

The trail leg, simultaneously, provides the second source of power. As the lead leg plants, the trail leg drives off the grass, extending the keeper into full flight. The keeper's centre of gravity travels both upward and outward — never just outward. A dive that is purely lateral, with no upward component, is what coaches call a fall. The keeper has dropped sideways under their own weight rather than launched themselves.

The hands lead the dive. They leave the set position before the feet finish their drive, and they reach for the ball at the moment of full extension. We will return to this in detail in Part IV, but the principle starts in the feet: the power step generates the energy that the hands deliver.

**Coaching cue.  **“Step to the corner.” Not “step to the side.” The forty-five degree angle is the difference between a dive that gets a hand on the ball and a dive that lands a metre short.

## Chapter 3 — Body Shape and Weight Transfer

The body shape of a saving goalkeeper changes constantly across the moment of the save. Five distinct shapes — set, loaded, extended, contacted, and recovered — follow each other in sequence within roughly six tenths of a second. The transitions between them are the technique.

### 3.1 — The Five Shapes of a Save

Every save in this encyclopedia, whether a routine catch or a full-extension dive, passes through some version of these five shapes. Understanding them in the abstract makes every specific technique easier to learn.

#### 1. Set.

Described in Chapter 1. Square chest, hinged hips, weight forward over the front of the boot, hands at waist, eyes on the ball. The body is in compression, like a spring loaded but not yet released.

#### 2. Loaded.

The brief moment after the shot has been struck and before the keeper has fully committed to a direction. The lead leg has begun to drive, the trail leg has begun to push, but the hands have not yet left the set position. The body is in transition. This phase lasts perhaps fifty milliseconds. It is invisible to the naked eye but visible on slow-motion video, and it is where many technical errors are exposed — the keeper who has shifted weight to the wrong leg in the set will not be able to load correctly here.

#### 3. Extended.

The body is in flight or in full reach. For a dive, this is the apex of the trajectory. For a catch, this is the moment the hands meet the ball. The shape varies enormously by save type — full extension on a top-corner dive, compact extension on a chest catch — but the principle is the same: the body has reached for the ball.

#### 4. Contacted.

The hands or body have made contact with the ball. The keeper is now in control of an outcome — catch, parry, deflect, block — and the technique shifts from saving the ball to managing the consequence. For a catch, this is the moment of grip and absorption. For a parry, this is the moment of redirection. For a block, this is the moment of impact and rebound management.

#### 5. Recovered.

The keeper is back on their feet, square to the next threat, and ready for the rebound or the second wave of attack. This shape is too often neglected in training. A goalkeeper who makes a brilliant save but cannot recover for the rebound is, in the brutal arithmetic of the match, identical to a goalkeeper who did not make the save at all.

### 3.2 — Weight Transfer

Weight in the set is forward — over the toes, over the front of the boot. Weight in the load is on the lead leg, the leg that will drive the dive. Weight in the extension is in flight, balanced through the core. Weight in the contact is into the ball — the keeper does not catch passively, they catch actively, with their weight moving toward the catch. Weight in the recovery is back over the feet.

The most common failure of weight transfer is leaving the weight on the trail leg during the load. The keeper attempts to drive off the wrong leg, the dive collapses, and the ball passes them at chest height. This error is invisible from behind the goal because the keeper appears to be moving in the right direction; it is only obvious when the camera is positioned to one side and you can see which leg the weight is sitting on at the moment of the shot.

**Coaching cue.  **“Load on the same side as the ball.” If the ball is going to the keeper's left, the weight loads onto the left leg before the dive begins. If the keeper has loaded onto the wrong leg, no power step in the world will save them.

## Chapter 4 — The Architecture of the Hands

There are five hand shapes a working goalkeeper coach must be able to teach, name, and correct on sight. They are the contour grip (W), the cup, the scoop, the chest catch, and the shock-absorption shape that underlies all parries. The hands of an elite goalkeeper move between these shapes constantly and unconsciously. The hands of a struggling goalkeeper get stuck in one shape and try to use it for everything.

### 4.1 — The Contour Grip (W-Catch)

**Use when**

Catching a ball at chest height or above, where the ball arrives in front of the body.

**Alternatives**

Cup (head height with no time to set hands), chest catch (chest height with body behind).

**Initiator**

Hands rise from the set with thumbs and index fingers forming the W.

**Most common failure**

Thumbs not touching — the ball passes between the hands and into the face.

#### Mechanics

The contour grip — known to most English-speaking coaches as the W-catch — is the fundamental hand shape for all catches above the waist. The thumbs touch behind the ball, forming the bottom of a W. The index fingers spread outward and upward at roughly forty-five degrees. The remaining fingers spread to fill the contour of the ball. The palms angle inward at roughly thirty degrees so that the hands form a shallow basket rather than two flat planes.

The wrists are slightly cocked back, locking the joint. The elbows are flexed at roughly one hundred and twenty degrees in the moment of contact, so that they can flex further on impact and absorb the ball's momentum. The hands do not snatch at the ball — they meet it, give a fraction of a centimetre, and then close. This is the meaning of the old phrase soft hands. Hands that are tense and locked will spill the ball; hands that are loose and uncommitted will be punched aside by the ball's force. Soft hands give exactly enough to absorb.

The contact point on the ball is the back third — not the back, not the side, but the upper-rear curve. If the hands meet the ball on its perfect equator, the catch is unstable; the ball will rotate forward or backward out of the hands. If the hands meet the ball too high on the upper hemisphere, the ball will skip downward. The back third is the geometrically correct contact zone.

**Coaching cue.  **“Thumbs together, eyes between them.” The keeper sights the ball through the gap formed by the W. If the thumbs separate, the gap opens, and the ball goes through it.

#### Common errors

Thumbs apart is the most dangerous error. A keeper who catches with thumbs separated by even a centimetre risks the ball squeezing through and striking them in the face — and the resulting flinch at the next high shot will compound into a permanent fear of catching. The fix is mechanical: drill the thumb position with the keeper holding a ball stationary in the W shape until the hands find it without thinking.

Hands too far in front of the body — what coaches call reaching — is the second-most-common error. A keeper who reaches for the ball cannot bring it back to the chest; they must catch and immediately stabilise it in space. This is not impossible but it is harder. The cue is catch in the window, meaning the imaginary plane that runs vertically from forehead to navel. The hands meet the ball in this window, then the ball is brought to the chest.

### 4.2 — The Cup

**Use when**

The ball arrives at head height and there is no time to form a full contour grip.

**Alternatives**

Contour grip (when there is time), tip-over (when the ball is rising above the bar).

**Initiator**

Hands rise as a unit, palms cupping toward each other.

**Most common failure**

Catching with palms flat — the ball rebounds off the heels of the hands.

#### Mechanics

The cup is a simpler, faster cousin of the contour grip. The palms face each other directly with a slight inward curve, like two halves of a bowl. The thumbs are alongside the index fingers rather than touching behind the ball. The catch is made with the palms and the inside surfaces of the fingers; the ball is then drawn down into the chest in a single motion.

The cup is the right hand shape when the contour grip would be too slow. It is a compromise — less secure than the W, but available faster. Senior keepers use the cup more than they use the W on shots above head height, because the time to form a full contour is not always available.

### 4.3 — The Scoop (Cradle)

**Use when**

Catching a ball that is rolling along or just above the ground.

**Alternatives**

Long barrier (when there is room and time), collapse save (when the ball is moving fast and bouncing).

**Initiator**

Hands drop with palms forward and fingers down.

**Most common failure**

Legs apart with no second barrier — the ball rolls between the keeper's legs.

#### Mechanics

The scoop is the foundational handling technique for ground balls. The keeper bends at the hips and knees so the head is over the ball. The hands drop in front of the legs, fingers pointing toward the ground, palms facing the oncoming ball. The little fingers nearly touch each other. The ball rolls into the palms; the forearms then sweep up and back, gathering the ball into the abdomen as the keeper straightens.

The legs must be together. This is the second barrier behind the hands. If the ball squirts through the hands — and at high speed on wet grass it sometimes will — the legs prevent it from reaching the goal. A keeper who scoops with legs apart, especially on a slippery pitch, has only one barrier, and the consequences when that barrier fails are severe.

There is a long-running debate among coaches about whether to scoop with feet flat together or with one knee dropped to the ground forming a long barrier. We will discuss the long barrier in detail in Chapter 35. For routine handling of a moderately paced ground ball, feet together is sufficient and faster to recover from. The long barrier is reserved for situations of higher risk: wet pitches, fast shots, shots arriving with deflection, and one-against-one situations where the ball is loose in front of an attacker.

### 4.4 — The Chest Catch

**Use when**

The ball arrives at chest height with the body already in line.

**Alternatives**

Contour grip (when the ball arrives in front of the body), abdominal catch (lower).

**Initiator**

Hands rise behind the ball; chest catches the ball into a basket of arms and torso.

**Most common failure**

Hands in front of the chest rather than behind — the ball rebounds off the hands.

#### Mechanics

The chest catch is the most secure handling technique in the goalkeeping repertoire. The body is squarely in line with the ball. The hands rise from the set position, fingers up, and meet the ball as it arrives at the chest, with the heels of the hands at sternum height and the palms forming a basket. The arms then enclose the ball against the chest in a single motion. Two barriers — the hands and the chest — are present, with the abdomen forming a third on slightly lower balls.

The shoulders must round forward at the moment of contact. A keeper who catches with the chest puffed out will have the ball rebound off the chest plate before the arms can close around it. The cue is hollow chest, meaning the breastbone draws backward toward the spine in the moment of contact, creating a soft surface that absorbs the ball rather than a hard one that returns it.

### 4.5 — Soft Hands and Shock Absorption

The principle of soft hands is the most-spoken and least-understood concept in goalkeeping. It does not mean weak hands. A goalkeeper with weak hands cannot hold a hard shot; the ball punches through the grip and into the face. Soft hands means hands that absorb.

The mechanism is in the elbows and the wrists. At the moment the ball strikes the hands, the elbows flex an additional ten to fifteen degrees and the wrists give backward by perhaps five degrees. This brief, controlled give absorbs the ball's kinetic energy and converts it into bend rather than rebound. The hands then immediately re-tense around the ball to secure the catch.

This sequence — meet, absorb, secure — is what separates a clean catch from a parry. A keeper who only meets and secures, without the absorb phase in the middle, will spill hard shots even when the hand position is technically correct. A keeper who absorbs without then securing will end up parrying balls they should be catching.

**Coaching cue.  **“Catch the ball, do not stop the ball.” Stopping the ball implies a hard surface; catching implies a yielding one. The goalkeeper's hands are an act of acceptance, not resistance.

## Chapter 5 — Reading the Play

Every save begins before the shot is struck. The goalkeeper who reads the play correctly arrives at the saving position with time to spare; the goalkeeper who reads it late arrives at full stretch. This chapter describes the small visual cues that elite goalkeepers process unconsciously and that lesser goalkeepers must be taught to look for.

### 5.1 — The Plant Foot

The shooter's plant foot — the non-kicking foot — is the single most reliable predictor of where the ball is going. The plant foot points in the direction of the shot. A right-footed striker who plants their left foot pointing toward the keeper's right post will, ninety per cent of the time, drive the ball toward that post. The plant foot is the rudder of the shot.

The keeper reads the plant foot in the half-second before contact. By the time the kicking leg comes through, the plant has already settled and committed the shooter to a direction. A keeper who is watching the ball — and only the ball — until the moment of contact has missed the most useful piece of information available.

Exceptions exist. Skilled strikers can disguise the plant or open up the body to fool the goalkeeper. But these are exceptions; the base rate strongly favours the plant foot as a predictor, and the keeper who reads it will be right far more often than they are wrong.

### 5.2 — The Hip Rotation

In the moment of contact, the shooter's hips rotate to deliver the kick. The direction and speed of this rotation are the second strongest predictor of shot placement. Hips that rotate fully and explosively produce powerful shots driven across the body, typically to the far post. Hips that stay closed produce shots redirected to the near post or down the middle.

The hip read is harder than the plant foot read because the rotation happens fast and is partially obscured by the kicking leg. It is the read of more senior keepers, layered on top of the plant-foot read for refinement.

### 5.3 — The Body Lean

The angle of the shooter's torso at the moment of contact predicts the height of the shot. A torso leaning backward produces an elevated shot; a torso leaning forward produces a low driven shot. A torso held vertically over the ball produces a flat, mid-height shot. This read is the easiest of the three and is often the first one a young keeper learns.

The combined read — plant foot for direction, hips for power, torso for height — is what allows elite goalkeepers to begin moving toward a shot before the shot has been struck. They are not guessing. They are reading data from the shooter's body that the shooter has unintentionally broadcast in the half-second before contact.

### 5.4 — Anticipation Versus Guessing

The line between anticipation — reading correctly and acting accordingly — and guessing — committing to a direction without reading — is the line between an elite goalkeeper and one who concedes preventable goals. Anticipation begins moving in the right direction and remains capable of correcting if the read was wrong. Guessing commits fully to one direction and is helpless if the read was wrong.

The technical difference is in the load. An anticipating keeper loads partially toward the predicted direction — perhaps shifting the weight thirty per cent toward the leg they expect to drive off — while remaining able to redirect. A guessing keeper has loaded one hundred per cent and cannot recover.

The teaching of anticipation begins by demanding that the keeper articulate, in training, what they read and why. After every shot in a finishing drill, ask the keeper: where did you think it was going, and what told you so? At first the keeper will not have an answer; they were watching the ball. After weeks of being asked, they will start watching the plant foot, the hips, the torso. After months, they will read the cues unconsciously, in the way an experienced driver reads the road.

**Coaching cue.  **“Read the body, not the ball.” The ball tells you nothing until it has been struck, and by then it is too late. The shooter's body has been telling you for half a second already.

# Part II — Catching and Handling

*To catch a ball cleanly is to win possession and end the threat in a single action. Every other outcome — parry, tip, punch, block — is a compromise made because catching was not possible. This part describes the catches in detail.*

*The catches are organised by the height and trajectory of the ball: ground, knee, abdomen, chest, head, and above the head. Each height demands a slightly different combination of footwork, body shape, and hand architecture. Most goalkeepers default to one comfortable height and grow weak at the others; this is the single most common pattern of weakness in semi-professional goalkeepers, and it is correctable.*

## Chapter 6 — The Ground-Ball Catch

The ground ball — a shot or pass arriving along or just above the surface — is the simplest catch in goalkeeping. It is also the one most commonly fumbled, because keepers underestimate it and stop attending to the technical details.

### 6.1 — The Standing Scoop

**Use when**

A ground ball arrives at moderate pace on a true surface.

**Alternatives**

Long barrier (faster shot, wet pitch), collapse (the ball is bouncing or dipping).

**Initiator**

Body bends at the hips with feet together; hands drop in front of the legs.

**Most common failure**

Bending at the waist instead of hinging at the hips — the head drops below the ball.

#### Mechanics

The keeper steps into line with the ball — square, feet together — and bends at the hips while keeping the spine relatively straight. The knees flex a moderate amount; this is not a deep squat. The hands drop in front of the legs, palms forward, fingers down, with the little fingers nearly touching. The ball rolls into the palms, the forearms sweep up and back, and the ball is gathered into the abdomen as the keeper straightens.

The eyes track the ball all the way into the hands. The single most common failure on a routine ground ball is the keeper looking up an instant before contact — a small, unconscious movement, but enough to cause the hands to be a few centimetres out of position when the ball arrives. Train the keeper to watch the ball into the abdomen, not into the hands.

**Coaching cue.  **“Hinge, do not bend.” The hinge is at the hips with a flat back. The bend is at the waist with a curved spine. The first is balanced and recoverable; the second is not.

### 6.2 — The Long Barrier

**Use when**

Ground balls on wet pitches, balls travelling at high speed, or balls with deflection or spin.

**Alternatives**

Standing scoop (faster recovery), collapse (when the ball is bouncing).

**Initiator**

One knee drops to the ground while the other knee remains up.

**Most common failure**

Dropping the wrong knee, leaving a gap to the goal between the down-knee and the goal post.

#### Mechanics

The long barrier is the safety-first variant of the ground-ball catch. The keeper steps into line and drops the knee on the goal-side leg to the ground, with the lower leg rotating outward so the shin and the inside of the calf form a continuous barrier from the up-knee to the down-foot. The up-knee — typically the inside knee, closer to the centre of the goal — remains flexed at roughly ninety degrees with the foot flat on the ground. The hands drop in front of the down-leg, palms forward, fingers down, just as in the standing scoop.

Three barriers are now present: the hands, the up-knee leg, and the long line of the down-leg. A ball that escapes the hands is stopped by the down-leg; a ball that bobbles or skips is caught by the up-knee. The keeper has dramatically reduced the probability of conceding from a routine ground ball.

The cost is recovery time. Getting up from a long barrier takes longer than getting up from a standing scoop, and during that time the keeper is less able to react to a rebound or a deflection. This is why the long barrier is reserved for higher-risk situations and is not the default. A goalkeeper who long-barriers every ground ball is slower than a goalkeeper who scoops the routine ones and reserves the barrier for when it is needed.

**Coaching cue.  **“Drop the goal-side knee.” On a ball coming from the right, the right knee drops; on a ball from the left, the left knee drops. The down-knee is always the knee on the side from which the ball is coming, so that the long line of the leg seals off the goal.

#### A note on tradition

In the English coaching tradition through the 1980s, the long barrier was the default ground-ball technique and was taught at every level. The Continental tradition — Italian, Dutch, German — preferred the standing scoop with feet together, citing the recovery cost. Modern professional coaching has converged on a hybrid: standing scoop as default, long barrier as a contextual choice. The keeper must be able to do both fluidly and choose the right one in real time.

### 6.3 — The Collapse Catch

**Use when**

A ground ball is bouncing unpredictably, or arriving fast and low at one side of the body.

**Alternatives**

Standing scoop (true surface), long barrier (room and time to set).

**Initiator**

The legs collapse outward as the body falls onto its side toward the ball.

**Most common failure**

Falling backward rather than sideways onto the ball — the keeper loses sight of it on the way down.

#### Mechanics

The collapse catch is described in greater detail in Part IV, where it appears as the lowest form of the diving family. In the catching context, the keeper folds the legs underneath the body and falls sideways toward the ball, with the lead hand going behind the ball and the trail hand going on top. The body lands on the side — outside of the leg, hip, ribs, and shoulder, in that order — with the ball secured against the abdomen by the trail hand pressing it into the lead hand.

The collapse is faster than the long barrier when the ball is moving fast, because it does not require the keeper to first set both feet. It is more secure than a one-handed deflection because both hands meet the ball. It is the workhorse technique for fast low shots that arrive within an arm's reach of the keeper's standing position.

## Chapter 7 — The Mid-Height Catch

Mid-height — between knee and chest — is the height of most direct shots from outside the box. It is also the height that exposes weak fundamentals most clearly, because a keeper has time to set, time to choose a hand shape, and time to make small errors visible.

### 7.1 — The Knee-Height Catch

**Use when**

A ball arrives at the keeper's knees with the body in line.

**Alternatives**

Scoop (lower), abdomen catch (higher), collapse (faster shot off the line).

**Initiator**

The keeper crouches, hands forming a basket below the body.

**Most common failure**

Catching with hands flat downward — the ball deflects off the heels of the hands and bounces into play.

#### Mechanics

The keeper crouches with the head over the ball, the chest hollowed forward, and the hands forming a basket immediately in front of the knees. The fingers point downward and slightly forward, with the palms angled to receive the ball. The catch is similar to a scoop but at a higher entry point. The ball is gathered into the abdomen by the same upward sweep of the forearms.

This catch is technically unstable for most goalkeepers because the entry height is awkward — too high for fingers down, too low for fingers up. Senior keepers solve this by keeping the fingers downward and bending the wrists more sharply backward, so the palms face the ball at a steep angle and the fingers cradle the underside. Junior keepers should be drilled into this position deliberately; left to their own devices they will catch with flat hands and the ball will bounce away.

### 7.2 — The Abdomen Catch

**Use when**

A ball arrives at the keeper's stomach with the body in line.

**Alternatives**

Chest catch (slightly higher), knee catch (lower).

**Initiator**

Hands rise slightly to receive the ball; abdomen contracts to absorb.

**Most common failure**

Receiving with the abdomen extended — the ball rebounds off the stomach.

#### Mechanics

The abdomen catch is the easiest catch in goalkeeping and the one most often muffed by inattention. The ball arrives at the stomach with the body in line. The hands meet the ball in front of the abdomen, fingers up, palms angled inward, and the abdomen draws backward at the moment of contact to absorb the ball into a soft cradle. The hands then enclose the ball against the stomach.

The error to watch for is a keeper standing too tall, with the abdomen pushed forward. The ball strikes a hard surface and rebounds. The cue is hollow your stomach — the abdominal muscles contract and the belly draws in slightly, creating a yielding surface.

### 7.3 — The Chest Catch

The chest catch is described in detail in Section 4.4 of Chapter 4. We add only one operational note here: the chest catch is the most secure handling outcome in the goalkeeper's repertoire and should be sought whenever the ball's trajectory permits it. A keeper who routinely chest-catches a ball that could have been caught at the chest with two hands is doing the right thing — the apparent inelegance of the chest catch is more than compensated for by its near-zero failure rate.

## Chapter 8 — The High Catch

Above the head, the keeper loses the secondary barrier of the body. The hands are alone. This is why catching above the head requires more technique, more confidence, and more training than any other handling family. It is also why the contour grip exists in the form it does.

### 8.1 — The Head-Height Catch

**Use when**

A ball arrives directly at the keeper's head height with the body in line.

**Alternatives**

Cup (less time), tip-over (the ball is rising over the bar).

**Initiator**

Hands rise from the set, thumbs converging behind the ball as the W forms.

**Most common failure**

Catching at face height instead of in front of it — the keeper flinches and the catch fails.

#### Mechanics

The contour grip described in Section 4.1 is the canonical hand shape for this catch. The hands rise from the set position, the thumbs touch behind the ball, the fingers spread, and the ball is met fifteen to twenty centimetres in front of the face. This forward catching distance is critical: a ball met directly at the face deprives the eyes of the visual reference and produces an instinctive flinch. A ball met out in front allows the eyes to track the ball through the catch.

The arms flex on contact, drawing the ball into the chest. The catch is then secured against the body in the same motion as a chest catch.

### 8.2 — The Above-Head Catch

**Use when**

Catching a high shot, cross, or corner above standing height.

**Alternatives**

Punch (under pressure or in traffic), tip-over (the ball is going over the bar).

**Initiator**

Drive off one foot, opposite knee up, hands extending fully overhead.

**Most common failure**

Catching with elbows locked out — no shock absorption available.

#### Mechanics

The above-head catch is the highest-risk handling action in goalkeeping. The ball is caught above the head, with the keeper in flight or at full extension, and there is no secondary barrier. The hands and the timing must be perfect.

The take-off is from one foot. The opposite knee drives upward to the chest — this knee both adds height to the jump and creates a protective barrier against an opposing player attempting to challenge through the keeper. The hands extend fully overhead, with the contour grip formed before the hands meet the ball. The ball is caught at the highest point of the jump, when the keeper is still rising or at the apex; never on the way down.

The elbows are not locked. They are flexed at roughly one hundred and fifty degrees, allowing for ten to fifteen degrees of further flexion on contact. This is the source of shock absorption. A keeper with locked elbows will spill the ball on contact; the rigid arms transmit the impact directly to the hands, which cannot give.

Once the catch is made, the ball is brought down to the chest immediately as the keeper descends. The arms wrap the ball against the body before the keeper lands. A keeper who lands and then secures the ball is exposed to a challenge during the landing phase; one who secures and then lands is not.

**Coaching cue.  **“Catch high, secure low.” The catch is at the highest point; the security is at the chest. There is no pause between the two — they are a single fluid motion.

### 8.3 — The Decision: Catch, Punch, or Tip

In a crowded penalty area, the keeper makes a high-ball decision before the cross arrives. This is a tactical decision, not a technical one, and it depends on three variables: pressure, trajectory, and distance.

If the keeper can reach the ball without contest, catch. The catch ends the threat and turns possession over.

If the keeper can reach the ball but only under contest from one or more attackers, punch. A clean catch under heavy pressure is high-risk; a punch is high-percentage. We will describe the punching technique in detail in Chapter 26.

If the ball is going to pass over or beyond the keeper's reach, tip. A tip-over the bar is described in Chapter 11. Beyond the keeper, the right action is often a backward retreat to defend the second phase rather than a desperate one-handed deflection.

The recent FIFA technical analysis of women's tournaments has documented a clear shift in this decision-making over the last few years: punches and clean catches are both increasing as percentages of high-ball outcomes, while tipping has decreased. This is a function of better positioning and better take-off technique rather than a change in the principles. When goalkeepers arrive at the cross in better position, more of the high balls become catchable or punchable, and fewer require the desperate tip.

# Part III — Parrying, Tipping, and Deflecting

*When a ball cannot be caught, it must be redirected. Where it is redirected to is a tactical choice the keeper makes in the fraction of a second between the shot and the contact. This part describes the techniques for redirecting balls that cannot or should not be caught.*

*Three families of redirection exist. The strong parry uses the palm and forearm to drive the ball away from danger with controlled power. The fingertip tip uses the ends of the fingers to redirect a ball with minimal contact, typically over the bar or around the post. The wrist deflection uses the back of the hand or the wrist to push a ball aside on a save where the front of the hand cannot get there. Each has a use, and each can be ruined by being attempted in the wrong situation.*

## Chapter 9 — The Strong Parry

The strong parry is the controlled, power-based redirection of a ball using the palm and the rigid wrist. It is the parry the keeper chooses when they have time to organise the hand and want to put the ball into a specific safe area.

### 9.1 — The Side Parry (Out for a Corner)

**Use when**

The ball cannot be caught and the keeper wants to drive it wide of the post.

**Alternatives**

Tip-over (when the ball is rising), catch (when contact is reachable).

**Initiator**

The hand extends with palm open and angled toward the corner flag.

**Most common failure**

Parrying back into the danger zone — the wrist angle was wrong.

#### Mechanics

The hand extends fully toward the ball, palm open, fingers spread, with the wrist locked at an angle that directs the rebound away from the goal and ideally beyond the post. This wrist angle is the technique. The palm is not flat; it is rotated so that the surface presented to the ball is angled at roughly forty-five to sixty degrees away from the line of the shot. The ball strikes the palm and rebounds along the angle of the palm — out for a corner, behind the goal, beyond the post.

The arm is firm but not locked. A locked arm transmits the full force of the shot back through the keeper's shoulder; a firm-but-flexed arm absorbs some of the impact and gives a more controlled deflection. The classic Italian description of this is la mano forte ma morbida — the strong but soft hand. Strong enough to redirect, soft enough to absorb.

The keeper's body finishes the dive in the same shape as a regular diving save: side-on landing on the outside of the leg, hip, and shoulder. The parrying hand follows through in the direction of the desired rebound — a follow-through that begins at the moment of contact and continues for ten or fifteen centimetres after the ball has left the hand. This follow-through is the difference between a parry that lands wide of the post and one that bounces back into the six-yard box.

**Coaching cue.  **“Show the corner flag.” The palm of the parrying hand should face the corner flag at the moment of contact. If the keeper can answer the question “where is your palm pointing?” correctly, the parry will go where it is supposed to go.

### 9.2 — The Forward Parry (Into Open Space)

Sometimes the right answer is not to put the ball wide but to drive it forward, into open space ahead of the goal where the keeper or a defender can recover it. This is the forward parry.

The mechanics are the same as the side parry except for the wrist angle. The palm faces forward and slightly upward, so that the ball rebounds away from the goal at a low trajectory and into open space rather than back into the danger zone. The forward parry is the right choice when the keeper is at full extension and cannot redirect the ball wide, but can still control the direction enough to send it away from goal.

The most common error in the forward parry is parrying the ball straight back into the path of an onrushing attacker. This happens when the keeper has not consciously chosen a direction for the parry; they have simply met the ball and let it rebound. A goalkeeper who parries without a destination is not parrying — they are absorbing impact and hoping. Train the keeper to call out the destination of every parry in training: corner, sideline, beyond the box, recovery space.

## Chapter 10 — The Tip-Over

The tip-over is the use of the fingers — sometimes a single hand, sometimes both — to redirect a ball over the crossbar. It is the highest-art technique in the parrying family, demanding the finest touch and the best-timed extension.

### 10.1 — The Two-Handed Tip

**Use when**

The ball is rising toward the upper portion of the goal and cannot be caught.

**Alternatives**

Catch (if reachable), single-fingertip (if only one hand can reach).

**Initiator**

Both hands rise, fingers extended, to meet the ball below its lower hemisphere.

**Most common failure**

Hitting the ball into the bar from below — insufficient height was available.

#### Mechanics

The keeper takes off from one foot for maximum vertical reach, opposite knee driving up. Both hands extend overhead, fingers up and palms facing the ball. The contact is made with the fingertips and the upper palms on the underside of the ball. The wrists snap back slightly at the moment of contact, redirecting the ball upward and backward — over the bar.

The ball does not need to be hit hard. A tip is a redirection, not a strike. The ball is already moving with significant kinetic energy; the keeper's job is to add a small upward vector. A keeper who attempts to punch the ball over the bar with a tip will mistime the contact and either miss the ball entirely or send it over with insufficient height, where it strikes the bar and drops back into play.

### 10.2 — The Strong-Side Single-Hand Tip

On a diving save toward the upper corner, the keeper often cannot reach the ball with two hands and must rely on one. The strong-side tip is the version performed with the lead hand — the hand on the same side as the dive.

The hand reaches the ball at full extension with fingers up, palm facing the ball and slightly angled upward. The fingertips contact the ball below its centre line, redirecting it upward and outward — over the bar, ideally with enough horizontal vector to clear the goal entirely rather than dropping back onto it.

The strong-side tip is the cleaner of the two single-hand options. The arm reaches naturally toward the ball, the wrist works in its strong direction, and the finger position is mechanically optimal. Most goalkeepers find this tip technically straightforward once the dive itself has been mastered.

### 10.3 — The Weak-Side Single-Hand Tip

The weak-side tip is the version performed with the trail hand — the hand opposite to the side of the dive. The keeper is reaching across their body to the ball; the wrist is working in its weak direction; the contact angle is awkward.

The mechanics are the same as the strong-side tip but the execution is harder. The keeper must rotate the shoulder forward to deliver the hand to the contact point, which costs reach. The wrist must snap in its less-natural direction, which costs power. And the body's flight path is less stable, because the lead arm is unable to balance the dive.

Despite these difficulties, the weak-side tip is a technique every senior goalkeeper must possess, because there are situations — particularly on shots that swerve in flight or on close-range deflections — where it is the only available action. Drill it deliberately. A weak-side tip that is good enough to keep the ball out of the goal is sufficient; it does not need to be elegant.

## Chapter 11 — The Wrist Deflection

The wrist deflection — using the back of the hand, the wrist itself, or the forearm — is the lowest-quality redirection in the parrying family and the one a keeper should resort to only when nothing else is available. It is, however, sometimes the only option, and a keeper who has not trained it will fail to make the redirection at all.

The technique is largely improvisational. The hand or wrist is presented in whatever orientation the keeper can manage, given the angle of approach and the limit of their reach. The ball is deflected by the surface that gets there in time. The keeper has minimal control over the direction of the rebound; their entire goal is to keep the ball out of the goal in this moment, and to deal with the consequences in the next moment by recovering quickly.

Train this as a last-resort skill. A goalkeeper who has wrist-deflected a ball over the bar with the back of their hand on a deflected shot has done their job. A goalkeeper who tries to wrist-deflect when a strong parry was available is making the wrong choice.

# Part IV — Diving Saves

*Diving is what goalkeeping looks like to the spectator. It is, in fact, only a small part of what goalkeeping is — most matches a senior keeper will dive perhaps three or four times — but it is the part that is photographed, and it is the part that decides the most consequential moments. This part describes diving by height and by direction, with the underlying principle that all dives share a common structure: take-off, flight, contact, landing.*

## Chapter 12 — Pedagogy: Teach Landing First

Before any keeper is taught to dive, they must be taught to land. The single most important principle in the teaching of diving saves is that the order of instruction is reverse to the order of execution: the keeper learns to land safely, then to fly, then to take off, then to combine all three.

A keeper who has not been taught to land will not commit fully to dives. They will pull up at the last moment, foreshortening the extension, fearing the impact with the ground. The result is a permanent ceiling on their dive range that no amount of physical training will fix until the fear is addressed.

### 12.1 — Landing Mechanics

The keeper lands on the side of the body — never on the back, never on the front, never on the elbow or knee. The contact sequence is: outside of the lower leg, outside of the thigh, hip, ribs, shoulder, in that order. The head is held up by an active locking of the neck muscles; this prevents the head from striking the ground if the body bounces. The free arm — the trail arm if the dive is to the lead side — comes across the body to absorb impact and protect the ribs.

The shoulder absorbs more impact than any other joint. A goalkeeper who lands on the elbow risks bone-bruise injuries that can sideline them for weeks; a goalkeeper who lands on the knee risks ligament damage. The shoulder and the outside of the upper arm, by contrast, are designed for impact and recover quickly.

The ground is not the enemy. A keeper who treats the ground as a hard, dangerous surface to be avoided will dive timidly. A keeper who treats it as a familiar surface — like a wrestler treats the mat or a judoka treats the tatami — will dive freely.

**Coaching cue.  **“Show me your hip.” The hip should hit the ground first among the load-bearing surfaces. If the keeper is showing me their elbow, knee, or back, the landing is wrong.

## Chapter 13 — The Dive Family

Dives are classified by height — collapse, low, mid, high — and by direction — lateral, diagonal-forward, backward. Each combination has its own mechanics. The Italian school orders the teaching progression as: collapse first, then low, then medium, then high. The forward diagonal is taught alongside the lateral. The backward dive is taught last and requires a level of comfort with the ground that takes years to develop.

### 13.1 — The Collapse Save (Caduta in Italian, valbeweging in Dutch)

**Use when**

A low ball arrives close to the keeper, within an arm's reach of the body.

**Alternatives**

Long barrier (more time and a true surface), low dive (the ball is further out).

**Initiator**

The legs collapse outward; the body folds and falls onto its side.

**Most common failure**

Collapsing backward onto the back rather than sideways onto the side.

#### Mechanics

The collapse is the smallest member of the dive family. The keeper does not fly; they fall. The legs fold outward — the leg on the side of the ball collapses, the opposite leg follows — and the body descends laterally. The hands lead the descent: the lead hand goes behind the ball, the trail hand on top. The body lands on the side, with the ball secured between both hands and the abdomen.

The collapse is the foundational dive technique because it teaches the keeper to commit to the ground without flying first. A keeper who can collapse correctly has solved the landing problem for every more advanced dive. The Italian coaching tradition spends weeks on the collapse before introducing the low dive, on the principle that a hundred good collapses are worth one good high dive in the foundation of a young keeper.

Modern Italian coaching identifies a particular variant called the rimozione della gamba — the leg-removal dive — in which the goal-side leg is actively swept out from under the body, accelerating the collapse. This is faster than a passive collapse and is preferred for shots arriving at speed.

### 13.2 — The Low Diving Save

**Use when**

A ball arrives at knee or ankle height beyond an arm's reach.

**Alternatives**

Collapse (closer), mid-height dive (higher trajectory).

**Initiator**

Power step at forty-five degrees, lead leg drives, trail leg pushes.

**Most common failure**

Diving across the body — the keeper passes over the ball rather than to it.

#### Mechanics

The low dive is the workhorse of professional goalkeeping. Most diving saves at senior level are low dives; the high spectacular saves are rare. The keeper power-steps with the lead leg at forty-five degrees toward the ball, the trail leg drives off the grass, and the body extends laterally and slightly forward. The hands lead — lead hand behind the ball, trail hand on top — and the contact is made roughly thirty centimetres above the ground.

Two principles govern the low dive. First, the dive is forward as well as sideways. The lead foot lands closer to the path of the ball than to the side of the body; if you photograph a low dive from above, the trajectory of the keeper is a diagonal toward the ball, not a perpendicular sideways path. Second, the body stays low. The keeper does not rise upward in the air and then descend to the ball; they travel along a low, flat trajectory parallel to the ground. The Dutch coaching cue for this is plat blijven — stay flat.

The lower hand is always the lead hand on a low dive. The trail hand presses the ball into the lead hand from above. This two-hand arrangement is what produces a clean catch on a low dive; a one-hand low dive will at best produce a parry, and the keeper has surrendered the secure outcome.

**Coaching cue.  **“Push the ground away.” The trail leg's drive is what sends the keeper into flight. If the trail leg is passive, the dive becomes a fall. The ground is being pushed away from the keeper's foot — the keeper does not rise toward the ball; the ground retreats from beneath them.

### 13.3 — The Mid-Height Diving Save

**Use when**

A ball arrives at chest or hip height beyond an arm's reach.

**Alternatives**

Step-and-catch (closer), high dive (higher trajectory).

**Initiator**

Power step with greater vertical component than the low dive.

**Most common failure**

Diving with the upper body leading instead of the hands — the keeper's chest meets the ball before the hands do.

#### Mechanics

The mid-height dive is a transitional technique between the low dive and the high dive. The power step is the same forty-five-degree drive, but the trail leg pushes more vertically, lifting the body roughly fifty to seventy centimetres off the ground at the apex of the flight. The hands lead — contour grip with thumbs touching — and the catch is made in front of the body at the height of the keeper's flight.

The upper body must not lead the dive. A keeper who throws the chest at the ball before the arms have extended will arrive at the ball with the elbows bent and the hands too close to the body. The catch fails because there is no shock-absorption distance between the hands and the chest. The cue is hands first — the hands precede the chest by perhaps thirty centimetres throughout the flight.

### 13.4 — The High Diving Save (The Power Save)

**Use when**

A ball is travelling toward the upper corner of the goal.

**Alternatives**

Tip-over (when the ball cannot be caught), step-and-tip (when there is time to take an extra step).

**Initiator**

Maximum drive from the trail leg; lead leg power-steps toward the post.

**Most common failure**

Insufficient horizontal extension — the keeper goes up but not out.

#### Mechanics

The high dive — also called the power save in many coaching traditions — is the most spectacular and the most physically demanding action in goalkeeping. The keeper drives off the trail leg with as much force as the legs can generate, sending the body simultaneously upward and outward. The lead leg's power step provides the lateral component; the trail leg's drive provides the vertical component. The two together produce the curved flight that takes the hands to the upper corner.

The hands extend fully overhead with the contour grip already formed. The catch is made at the apex of the flight or at full extension, whichever comes first. On a top-corner shot, the catch is rarely clean — the contact is usually a parry over the bar or around the post — but on a slightly less ambitious shot to the upper third of the goal, a clean catch is possible and should be the keeper's goal.

The body's orientation in flight is roughly horizontal at the moment of contact — the keeper is lying on their side in the air, parallel to the ground, with the lead arm fully extended. This horizontal orientation is generated by the relative angles of the two leg drives and is one of the technical hallmarks of a well-trained high dive. A keeper whose body remains diagonal in flight has not generated enough lateral force; one whose body is fully horizontal but at insufficient height has not generated enough vertical force. The two must be in proportion.

### 13.5 — The Diagonal-Forward Dive

The diagonal-forward dive is the dive toward a ball that is travelling away from the keeper as well as laterally — typically a shot from outside the box that has been deflected, or a shot delivered with significant forward pace from a close-range position. The keeper must travel both forward and sideways to meet the ball.

The power step is at less than forty-five degrees — perhaps thirty degrees — sending the keeper more forward than sideways. The trail leg's drive remains vertical. The hands extend forward of the body to meet the ball at a point further from the goal line than a lateral dive would reach. The flight path is more diagonal-forward than parallel to the goal line.

The diagonal-forward dive is the technique that connects routine shot-stopping to the sweeper-keeper actions described in Part VII. A keeper who can diagonal-forward dive aggressively can intercept through balls and dink shots in front of the goal that a keeper limited to lateral dives cannot reach.

### 13.6 — The Backward Dive (Recovery)

**Use when**

The ball has been struck or deflected over the keeper's head and is heading toward the goal.

**Alternatives**

Drop-step and recover (when there is time), turn-and-run (when the ball is going to the bar).

**Initiator**

The keeper drives off both feet with the body twisting in flight.

**Most common failure**

Failing to twist — the keeper falls backward onto their back without recovering shape.

#### Mechanics

The backward dive is the rarest dive in goalkeeping and the hardest to teach. The keeper drives off both feet with a slight backward lean and twists in flight so that the body lands sideways rather than flat on the back. The hands reach overhead and behind, attempting to push the ball over the bar or, if it is travelling too fast, simply to deflect it onto the bar.

The twist in flight is the technical key. Without it, the keeper lands flat on the back, which is a hard impact on the spine and an awkward recovery. With the twist, the keeper lands on the side, which is the same safe landing as any other dive.

Drill the backward dive only with goalkeepers who have already mastered all other dive directions. It is high-risk, high-reward, and a keeper who attempts it without adequate preparation will hurt themselves.

## Chapter 14 — Recovery and the Second Save

A goalkeeper who makes a brilliant save and concedes from the rebound has, in the language of match analysis, made one save and conceded one goal. The arithmetic does not credit the brilliance. This chapter describes how to recover from a save in time to make the next one.

### 14.1 — Getting Up

The fastest way to get up from the ground is from the side, using the lower hand to push and the lower leg to drive. The keeper rolls slightly toward the chest, plants the lower hand, and presses upward in a single motion that simultaneously brings the legs underneath the body. The keeper arrives in a low set position facing the ball.

The slowest way to get up — and the most common — is to roll onto the back, then sit up, then stand. This sequence is perhaps a full second slower than the side-push recovery, and a full second is enough for a rebound to become a goal.

### 14.2 — Tracking the Rebound

On a parry rather than a catch, the keeper's eyes never leave the ball. As the ball leaves the hand and the keeper begins the landing, the head is already turning — slightly, controlled — to track the trajectory of the parry. By the time the keeper has landed, the eyes are already locked onto the ball wherever it has gone, and the recovery from the ground is oriented toward that ball.

This eye-discipline is what allows a keeper to make a second save on a parried shot. A keeper who closes the eyes during contact, or who looks down at the ground during the landing, will be a fraction of a second behind the play when they recover, and that fraction is decisive.

# Part V — Aerial Work

*The penalty area is the keeper's domain, and within it the air is theirs to command. The cross, the corner, the long set-piece delivery, the chipped through ball — all of these test the keeper's ability to dominate space in three dimensions. Aerial work is where the goalkeeper most visibly takes ownership of the box, and it is where the difference between an average keeper and a great one is most obvious to the watching teammate.*

*The technical demands are fewer than in shot-stopping but the consequences of failure are greater. A misjudged cross usually leads directly to a goal; a misjudged shot usually leads to a parry that can be recovered. Aerial work is therefore the area where the highest-stakes decisions in goalkeeping are made.*

## Chapter 15 — Reading the Cross

Before any aerial action is taken, the keeper must read the cross. The reading begins the moment the crosser plants the standing foot and continues until the keeper has committed to a course of action. Three pieces of information are extracted from the crosser's body: the foot used, the angle of the body, and the part of the foot that contacts the ball.

### 15.1 — In-Swinger or Out-Swinger

A right-footed crosser delivering from the right wing produces an out-swinger — the ball curves away from the goal as it travels. The same crosser delivering from the left wing produces an in-swinger — the ball curves toward the goal. The mirror image applies for a left-footed crosser.

The reading is therefore mechanical: foot used, side of the field, equals known curve direction. A keeper who has internalised this reads the curve before the ball has been struck and is already adjusting position. A keeper who has not will be surprised by the curve in flight, and will arrive at the catch point a half-metre out of position.

The implications for positioning are direct. On an in-swinger, the keeper's starting position is slightly closer to the near post — the ball will curve toward goal and the keeper must be prepared to come for the front-post area. On an out-swinger, the starting position is slightly deeper and more central — the ball will curve away and the keeper has time and space to attack the ball in the middle of the goal area.

### 15.2 — Trajectory and Pace

Within the in-swinger or out-swinger family, the specific trajectory is determined by the contact point on the ball and the speed of the kicking foot. A driven cross — struck through the lower middle of the ball — travels flat and fast, arrives in the area within a second, and demands an immediate decision. A lofted cross — struck through the lower portion with the foot opening — travels high and slowly, gives the keeper more time, but also more uncertainty as the ball hangs in the air.

Driven crosses are the harder of the two for a keeper because the time to decide is shorter. Lofted crosses are technically easier but expose the keeper to a longer period of pressure from attackers in the box, and the dropping ball at the end of a high trajectory is itself difficult to time.

### 15.3 — The Decision Triangle

As the cross is struck, the keeper makes a decision among three options: come for the ball, stay on the line, or hold the position and prepare for a shot. The decision is made within roughly one second.

Come for the ball when: the trajectory will land within the keeper's catchable zone (a roughly six-metre rectangle in front of the goal); no attacker is closer to the predicted landing point than the keeper; the keeper has a clear path through traffic. The cue, drilled by Eric Steele and others, is last to leave, first to the ball — the keeper does not move early, but once they move, they move with full commitment.

Stay on the line when: the trajectory is to the back of the box or beyond the keeper's reach; an attacker is closer to the landing point and will get there first; the keeper's path through traffic is blocked. The keeper holds the line position, gets set, and prepares for the headed or volleyed shot.

Hold the position when: the cross is short and will be intercepted by a defender; the cross is long and will go beyond the goal; the trajectory is unclear and the keeper needs another half-second to read it. This third option is what Continental coaches sometimes call the read-and-react stance, and it is a legitimate choice that should not be confused with indecision. The keeper has chosen, briefly, to wait.

**Coaching cue.  **“Decide loud.” The keeper announces the decision the moment it is made — Keeper! to come, Away! to stay, or no call to hold. Defenders need to hear the decision so they know whether to clear the area in front of the keeper or to defend the headed shot.

## Chapter 16 — The Take-Off

The take-off determines the height the keeper reaches and the protection they have during the catch. The two basic take-offs — one foot and two feet — are used in different situations and produce different mechanics.

### 16.1 — The One-Foot Take-Off

**Use when**

Maximum height is required and the keeper has space to run into the jump.

**Alternatives**

Two-foot take-off (when the keeper is stationary at the moment of jump).

**Initiator**

Drive off the lead foot; opposite knee drives upward to the chest.

**Most common failure**

Driving off the wrong leg — the keeper goes up but not toward the ball.

#### Mechanics

The one-foot take-off is the higher of the two jumps. The keeper takes one or two running steps toward the catch point, plants the lead foot — typically the left foot for a right-handed catcher attacking the ball with the right hand — and drives upward. Simultaneously, the opposite knee drives forcefully toward the chest. This knee drive does three things: it adds upward momentum to the jump, it raises the centre of mass of the body, and it provides a defensive barrier against opposing players who might challenge through the keeper.

The arms extend overhead as the keeper rises. The contour grip is formed before the hands meet the ball. The catch is made at the apex of the jump, at the highest point the keeper can reach, and never on the way down. A keeper who catches on the descent has missed the apex and exposed themselves to being beaten in the air.

The landing on a one-foot take-off is on both feet, knees flexed to absorb the impact, with the ball brought down to the chest before the feet touch the ground. The keeper does not land with the ball still overhead; the descent and the secure-to-chest motion are simultaneous.

### 16.2 — The Two-Foot Take-Off

The two-foot take-off is used when the keeper is stationary at the moment the cross is delivered and does not have space to run into the jump. The keeper drives off both feet equally, the opposite knee may still drive up for protection, and the catch is made at the apex of a slightly lower jump than the one-foot variant produces.

This take-off is also the right choice in tight, crowded areas where a running approach is not possible. The keeper has less height available but more stability, and the increased stability is sometimes the right trade in a packed six-yard box.

### 16.3 — The Knee Drive

On either take-off, the lifted knee is the keeper's protection against challenge. A goalkeeper who jumps with both legs hanging is exposed to being knocked off balance by an attacker arriving with momentum. A goalkeeper who jumps with the opposite knee driven up to chest height presents a hard, raised surface that an attacker cannot push through without committing a foul.

The knee should be raised even when there is no apparent challenge. The knee drive is part of the technique, not an optional defensive add-on, and a keeper who does not drive the knee on every aerial attack is one challenge away from a problem.

## Chapter 17 — Punching the Cross

When catching is not the right choice, punching is. The punch is the controlled, powerful redirection of an aerial ball using one or both fists, designed to send the ball as far as possible from the keeper's goal.

### 17.1 — The Single-Fist Punch

**Use when**

Reaching beyond the keeper's catching range, or punching with one hand to redirect the ball at a specific angle.

**Alternatives**

Two-fist punch (more power), tip-over (when the ball is rising).

**Initiator**

Lead fist drives upward and through the ball with elbow extension.

**Most common failure**

Punching with a soft wrist — the ball deflects rather than travels.

#### Mechanics

The single-fist punch uses the lead hand only. The fist is closed firmly, with the fingers tucked tightly against the palm and the thumb wrapped over the index and middle finger. The contact surface is the flat front of the knuckles, not the curve of the back of the hand. The wrist is locked rigidly in line with the forearm; a flexed wrist will collapse on contact and absorb the force that should have been transferred to the ball.

The punching motion is short and direct — what the great Bob Wilson called the piston punch. The elbow extends explosively, driving the fist upward and through the ball. The arm does not swing; it pistons. A long swinging arm motion is slow, easy to mistime, and prone to glancing contacts. A short piston motion is fast, accurate, and produces solid contact.

Contact is made through the lower half of the ball — slightly below centre — to send the ball upward and away. The follow-through continues for ten or twenty centimetres past the contact point in the direction the keeper wants the ball to travel.

**Coaching cue.  **“Hit the ball, do not push it.” The hit is a short explosive piston; the push is a long slow swing. The two feel completely different to the keeper and look completely different on video.

### 17.2 — The Two-Fist Punch

The two-fist punch — sometimes called boxing the ball — is the maximum-power redirection. Both fists meet the ball simultaneously, knuckles together, with the keeper's arms forming a V at the moment of contact. The arms drive forward and upward in a piston motion, and the contact is made through the lower portion of the ball.

The two-fist punch is the right choice when maximum distance is the priority and accuracy is secondary — typically on a cross under heavy pressure where the keeper needs to clear the box entirely. It is harder to direct precisely than a single-fist punch, but it produces more force and a longer clearance.

The fists must be together at the moment of contact, not just close. A gap between the fists exposes the keeper to the ball squirting between the hands and continuing toward the goal. The hands form a single contact surface; this is the meaning of boxing the ball.

### 17.3 — Punch Direction and Distance

A punch sends the ball in the direction the fist is travelling. This sounds obvious, but it is the principle most commonly forgotten under pressure. A keeper who punches the ball with the fist moving sideways will send the ball sideways. A keeper who punches with the fist moving forward and upward will send the ball forward and up — out of the box, ideally toward the touchline.

The aim of every punch is to clear the eighteen-yard line. A punch that lands inside the penalty area has not removed the threat; it has merely delayed it. In training, set up cones on the eighteen-yard line and demand that punches clear them. A keeper who consistently punches inside the box is choosing a low-quality outcome and is at risk of the second-phase goal.

## Chapter 18 — The Tip Over the Bar from a Cross

On a cross that is rising too high to catch and too far back to punch, the right action is to tip the ball over the bar. This is mechanically the same as the tip-over described in Chapter 10, but performed in the context of a crowded box rather than a one-against-one shot-stopping situation.

The keeper takes off from one foot for maximum height, opposite knee driving up, and reaches with both hands to the underside of the ball. The fingertips contact the lower hemisphere and redirect the ball upward and backward over the bar. The body is in flight beneath the ball; the keeper lands behind their starting position and immediately gets set for a second-phase action — a corner, typically.

This tip is a defensive last resort on crosses, and a keeper who routinely tips crosses they could have caught or punched is leaving cheap possession with the opposition. But on a cross that has gone wrong — over the keeper's reach, over the goal area, dropping toward the top corner — the tip is the difference between conceding a goal and conceding a corner.

## Chapter 19 — Communication on Crosses

The aerial action is shared between the goalkeeper and the defenders. The keeper's verbal calls coordinate the action and prevent the most dangerous error in defensive set-piece play: two defenders going for the same ball while a third goes unmarked.

### 19.1 — The Calls

The keeper has three primary calls and uses them at full volume. Keeper! means the goalkeeper is coming for the ball and all defenders should clear the catch zone. Away! means the goalkeeper is staying and the defenders should attack the ball. Hold! or Set! means everyone holds position and prepares for the second-phase shot.

The calls are made the moment the decision is made — typically within the first half-second of the cross's flight. Late calls are worse than no calls; a defender hearing keeper! at the last instant has already committed to attacking the ball and cannot back out. Early calls are better than late calls. Loud calls are better than quiet calls. There is no advantage to subtlety.

### 19.2 — The Pre-Set-Piece Plan

Before the corner is delivered, the goalkeeper has already had a conversation with the defenders. Who is marking which attacker. Who is on the near post and back post. Where the keeper will start. What the calls will be on different deliveries. This conversation takes ten seconds and prevents most of the disasters that occur on defensive set pieces.

The goalkeeper is the only player on the pitch who can see the entire defensive shape from the goal line. They are responsible for organising the shape and noticing when it is wrong. A keeper who does not direct the defence on set pieces is leaving fifty per cent of their value at the dressing-room door.

# Part VI — One-Against-One and Close Range

*The one-against-one is the most decisive moment in goalkeeping. It is also the moment most poorly taught, because the techniques have evolved faster than coaching education has kept up. Twenty years ago every keeper was taught to come out fast and dive at the attacker's feet. Today the elite keepers stay on their feet, close space with footwork, and use a body shape borrowed from futsal that did not exist in the European coaching syllabus a generation ago.*

*This part describes the techniques in their modern form, with the older techniques presented for context where they remain useful. The keeper who is fluent in all of them — and who can choose between them in real time — is the keeper who turns one-against-ones into routine saves rather than into goals.*

## Chapter 20 — Narrowing the Angle

Before any one-against-one technique is executed, the angle must be narrowed. The keeper advances from the goal line to a position where their body covers the maximum possible portion of the goal mouth from the attacker's perspective. This advance is the foundation; the technique that follows is built on top of it.

### 20.1 — Into the Line

Into the line means the keeper advances along the imaginary line that runs from the centre of the ball through the centre of the goal. From this line, the keeper's body equally bisects the angle the attacker is shooting into. Standing on this line, the keeper has covered as much of the goal as their body and arm-span allow.

The position on the line — how far forward — is the second decision. Coming further forward narrows the angle further but increases the keeper's vulnerability to a chip and reduces their reaction time. Coming less far forward gives more reaction time but covers less of the goal. The right balance depends on the situation: an attacker with a heavy first touch can be advanced on aggressively; an attacker who has just taken a good touch is harder to advance on without becoming exposed to the chip.

### 20.2 — Down the Line

Down the line is the term Continental coaches use for the lateral component of angle narrowing. The keeper is not just on the line between the ball and the goal — they are also at the right depth on it. As the ball moves laterally — across the field, around a defender, between attacking players — the keeper adjusts laterally in step with the ball, maintaining their position on the line.

The footwork for this lateral adjustment is the side-step described in Chapter 2. Small fast shuffles, body square, hands at waist, eyes on the ball. The keeper's lateral movement is in continuous motion with the ball, not in stopped-and-restarted increments.

## Chapter 21 — The Set-Set Approach

Described briefly in Chapter 1, the set-set approach is the modern technique for closing space in a one-against-one. The keeper advances toward the attacker in small fast steps, getting set on each touch the attacker takes, and explodes forward again the moment the ball leaves the attacker's foot.

This approach has replaced the older head-down rush that was standard a generation ago. The advantage of the set-set is adjustability. A keeper who is set on each touch is able to react to a shot, a chip, or a continuation of the dribble. A keeper rushing forward at full speed has committed to a single moment of contact and cannot adjust if the attacker does not shoot in that moment.

### 21.1 — Step Length and Cadence

The steps in a set-set approach are short — perhaps thirty to forty-five centimetres each — and their cadence is matched to the attacker's touches. If the attacker is dribbling at a fast cadence, the keeper's steps are at the same cadence. If the attacker has slowed to take a longer touch, the keeper slows correspondingly.

The steps shorten as the keeper closes the gap. At the start of the approach, when the attacker is fifteen metres out, the steps may be larger and the keeper may even break into a brief jog. As the gap closes to ten metres, the steps shorten to the standard set-set rhythm. Inside seven metres, the steps are very small — perhaps fifteen centimetres each — and the keeper is essentially shuffling forward in a low set.

### 21.2 — The Body Shape Through the Approach

Throughout the approach, the body remains in the low set described in Chapter 1. Hips dropped, chest tall, hands at mid-thigh height with palms forward. The keeper does not stand up between sets to take longer steps; the low position is held throughout. This is exhausting in training, and a keeper who has not trained the legs to hold the low set under fatigue will stand up unconsciously toward the end of the approach. Train this until it is automatic.

## Chapter 22 — The K-Barrier (The Spanish K)

The K-barrier is the modern signature technique of one-against-one defending and the single most important addition to the goalkeeping syllabus in the last twenty years. It originated in futsal — where its inventors are credited variously to Spanish, Portuguese, and Brazilian coaches working in the indoor game — and migrated to outdoor football through the Spanish and Portuguese national teams in the early 2000s.

### 22.1 — The Shape

**Use when**

An attacker is bearing down on the keeper at close range and the keeper has chosen to stay on their feet.

**Alternatives**

Smother (the attacker has taken a heavy touch), block save (final-instant choice), starfish (point-blank).

**Initiator**

Lead foot steps wide; opposite knee drops toward the inside of the ankle.

**Most common failure**

Going to ground too early — the K is a standing technique, not a sliding one.

#### Mechanics

The keeper's lead leg — the leg on the side of the ball — steps wide, with the foot turning outward roughly forty-five degrees. The knee remains flexed but stays high. The opposite knee drops downward and inward, hovering just above the ankle of the lead leg without touching the ground. The hips stay low and open, presenting a wide base. The chest stays tall and square to the attacker. The arms spread out wide and downward, with the hands held at roughly knee height, palms facing the attacker. From the attacker's perspective, the keeper presents a barrier shaped like the letter K — the upright of the lead leg, the diagonal of the dropped knee, and the horizontal of the spread arms.

This shape covers the maximum possible silhouette in front of the goal while keeping the keeper on their feet and capable of reacting. It denies the easy chip — the chest is tall — and the easy dribble through the legs — the legs form a wide low base. It denies the corner shot — the arms are wide. The only shots that beat it are perfectly placed shots into the upper corners, which are difficult under one-against-one pressure.

### 22.2 — Why Standing Beats Sliding

The older technique — sliding feet-first or hands-first at the attacker's feet — has two structural disadvantages. First, it commits the keeper to a single moment of contact. If the attacker does not deliver the ball into that moment, the keeper is on the ground and helpless. Second, it gives away a penalty if the keeper does not get a touch on the ball; a tackle from a goalkeeper that misses the ball and connects with the attacker is a foul, regardless of intent.

The K-barrier solves both problems. The keeper is on their feet and can adjust if the attacker dribbles, takes another touch, or attempts to round the keeper. There is no all-or-nothing moment of commitment. And the keeper has not committed to a tackle, so the foul risk is dramatically reduced.

This is why the K-barrier has become the dominant technique at elite level. Bill Hamid, Steve Clark, and a generation of European keepers raised in the modern coaching tradition use it as their default one-against-one technique. The slide remains useful in specific situations — described below — but it is no longer the first choice.

### 22.3 — The K-Barrier and the Save

From the K-barrier, the actual save is a small additional movement. If the shot is low and central, the keeper drops the body slightly to absorb it with the chest and abdomen. If the shot is to the corner, the keeper extends the lead arm or leg in that direction — a quick collapse-style save that begins from the K position. If the attacker chips, the keeper rises and arches backward to redirect the ball over the bar.

The saves from the K-barrier are not flashy. They are small, contained, controlled. They look almost easy on video. This is the point. A goalkeeper who has positioned correctly does not need to dive spectacularly; they need to be in the right shape at the right time.

## Chapter 23 — The Long Barrier (1v1 Variant)

The long barrier described in Chapter 6 has a one-against-one variant. When the attacker is dribbling toward the keeper at close range and the keeper has chosen to drop to the ground, the long barrier is one of the available shapes.

The keeper drops the goal-side knee — the knee on the side closest to the goal — to the ground, with the lower leg extended along the line of the shot to form a long barrier. The opposite knee remains up. The hands drop in front of the down-leg, palms forward. The body is now a shape that occupies the lower portion of the goal mouth from the attacker's perspective, with the hands ready to receive a low shot and the long line of the leg as a backstop.

This is a less aggressive technique than the K-barrier. It surrenders the upright posture and the ability to react to a chip, in exchange for maximum coverage of the lower portion of the goal. It is the right choice when the keeper is confident the attacker will shoot low — typically because they have closed space and there is no chip-able height available — and wants to commit to taking that shot away.

## Chapter 24 — The Smother

The smother is the keeper's response to a heavy touch from the attacker. The ball is loose, the attacker is reaching for it, and the keeper has the chance to dive in and gather the ball before the attacker can reach it.

The technique is a forward-diagonal dive. The keeper power-steps with the lead leg toward the loose ball, the trail leg drives, and the body extends forward and downward. The hands lead — both hands, contour grip or scoop grip depending on the height of the ball — and the body lands on top of the ball, securing it against the chest and abdomen.

The keeper must reach the ball before the attacker. This is a binary outcome; if the keeper does not arrive first, the smother becomes a foul or a missed touch and the attacker scores. The decision to smother is therefore a high-confidence decision: only smother when the touch was heavy enough to give the keeper a clear time advantage. A marginal smother is a penalty waiting to happen.

**Coaching cue.  **“Beat the boot.” The keeper's hands must reach the ball before the attacker's foot. If there is any doubt, do not smother. The K-barrier was designed for exactly this uncertainty.

## Chapter 25 — The Starfish (Star Jump, Spread)

The starfish is a point-blank technique used when an attacker has the ball at the keeper's feet and is in the act of shooting from inside three metres. There is no time for any other technique; the keeper has perhaps a tenth of a second to act.

The keeper jumps and spreads — arms fully extended outward and slightly upward, legs spread sideways at roughly forty-five degrees, body forming an X-shape in the air. The aim is to occupy the maximum possible silhouette in front of the goal in the moment the attacker shoots, in the hope that the ball strikes some part of the keeper's body.

This is not a technique — it is a tactic. The keeper has surrendered control over what happens to the ball; they are simply maximising the chance of any contact at all. Peter Schmeichel, who is most associated with this shape in the English-speaking tradition, used it sparingly and only at extreme close range. Misused — at distances where a more controlled technique would have worked — the starfish is high-risk and concedes far more than it saves.

Train it as an emergency option. Drill the jump, the spread, the absence of fear. But teach the keeper that it is the last technique, not the first.

## Chapter 26 — The Block Save

The block save is the keeper's response to a shot taken at very close range when there is no time to dive or to organise a K-barrier. It is the technique David de Gea built much of his career around, and it has become a standard tool in the modern keeper's repertoire.

### 26.1 — The Shape

From a low set position, with the attacker shooting from within five or six metres, the keeper's nearer knee — the knee on the side of the near post — rises up to stay high. The opposite knee drops downward to the inside of the lead foot, but does not touch the ground. The chest stays tall, perpendicular to the shooter. The arms spread wide and downward, palms facing the shooter.

This shape is similar to the K-barrier in its silhouette, but it is a reactive technique rather than an approach technique. The K-barrier is held while the keeper is closing space; the block save is adopted in the instant before contact, often as the only available response when a closer technique has failed or has been pre-empted.

### 26.2 — Reading the Block

The block save is read off the attacker's body the same way any shot is read. The plant foot, the hip rotation, the body lean — all of these tell the keeper where the shot is going and at what height. The keeper's block is then organised to cover the predicted shot direction. The near-post knee covers the near-post low corner; the far arm covers the far-side mid-height; the body covers the centre.

De Gea's signature variant of this technique used the legs as the primary saving tool, blocking shots with the boots and shins rather than the hands. This is futsal-derived — in futsal the foot block is the standard technique because the ball is at foot level and the goal is small — and it works in football when the angle and the shot height permit it. A keeper trained in this variant has access to a save technique with two more limbs than the hands-only goalkeeper has.

# Part VII — Sweeper-Keeper Actions

*The high defensive line of the modern game has produced a new kind of goalkeeper. They start their resting position not on the line, not even on the six-yard box, but on the eighteen-yard line — sometimes beyond it. They sweep behind the back line, intercept through balls outside the box, and contribute to the team's pressing structure as effectively as any outfield defender. Manuel Neuer is the canonical example, but the techniques are now standard at every elite club, taught from youth-academy level upward.*

*This part describes the techniques of sweeping. They are mechanically distinct from shot-stopping and from aerial work; they share more with the techniques of an outfield defender than with the techniques of a traditional goalkeeper. A keeper who has not trained these techniques will be lost the first time a ball is played in behind a high line, and the resulting goal will be entirely preventable.*

## Chapter 27 — The Starting Position

Before any sweeping action can be taken, the keeper must be in the right starting position. The principle is simple: the keeper's depth depends on the depth of their team's defensive line, and they are positioned to cover the space behind the line.

### 27.1 — Three Reference Heights

Coaches refer to three reference heights for the keeper's resting position. The line — the keeper is on or just in front of the goal line, used in low blocks and during sustained pressure. The six — the keeper is on the edge of the six-yard box, used in mid-blocks and in standard defensive shape. The eighteen — the keeper is on or near the edge of the eighteen-yard box, used when the team is pressing high or when the defensive line is set high up the pitch.

The keeper moves between these heights continuously throughout a match. As the ball advances toward the keeper's goal, the keeper retreats. As the team wins the ball and pushes upfield, the keeper advances. The transitions are quick and continuous; a keeper who is slow to advance after a turnover is leaving the back line exposed to a counter-attack.

### 27.2 — The Read of the Through Ball

The keeper reads through balls before they are played. The cues are familiar from shot-stopping but applied at greater distance: the body shape of the player on the ball, the open space behind the defensive line, the runs of attacking players. A keeper who has read the through ball can be advancing toward its predicted landing point before the pass has been struck. A keeper who has not will arrive at the ball half a second late, and that half-second is the difference between a clean interception and a one-against-one.

## Chapter 28 — The Outfield Clearance

When the keeper has advanced to intercept a through ball outside the penalty area, they must use their feet. The hands are no longer available. The technique becomes that of an outfield defender.

### 28.1 — The Standing Clearance

**Use when**

The keeper has time to set the body and clear the ball with control.

**Alternatives**

Slide clearance (less time), header (high ball).

**Initiator**

Plant foot beside the ball; striking foot drives through the lower half.

**Most common failure**

Slicing the ball — contact was made with the inside of the boot rather than the laces.

#### Mechanics

The standing clearance is a defensive kick. The keeper plants the non-kicking foot beside the ball, points the plant foot in the direction the ball is to be cleared, and drives the kicking foot through the lower half of the ball with the laces. The body leans slightly back to elevate the trajectory. The ball travels high and far, ideally to the touchline rather than down the centre of the field.

The contact point is the laces — the hardest, most stable part of the boot. Contact with the inside of the boot produces a controlled pass, which is rarely what the keeper wants in this situation. Contact with the toe produces an unpredictable trajectory and is often the cause of slices and miss-hits. The laces, struck through the lower half of the ball, produce a clean defensive clearance.

The keeper does not need to clear with maximum power on a sweeping action. The aim is space and time — getting the ball away from the danger zone and giving the team room to reorganise. A controlled clearance to the wing is more valuable than a powerful clearance straight back to the opposing centre-backs.

### 28.2 — The Slide Clearance

When the keeper is racing the ball and the attacker simultaneously, and there is no time to set up a standing clearance, the slide is the alternative. The keeper extends one leg toward the ball — the lead leg — with the toe pointing toward the destination. The opposite leg folds underneath. The keeper's body lands on the side, with the contact made through the lead leg as it slides through the ball.

The slide is high-risk. A miss-timed slide leaves the ball available for the attacker, the keeper on the ground, and the goal undefended. It is also outside the penalty area, where the keeper has no hand privileges and is subject to the same fouling rules as any defender. A slide that catches the attacker's leg without connecting with the ball is a foul, and a foul on the last defender outside the box is potentially a red card.

Train the slide as a precise tool. The aim is to make contact with the ball cleanly through the centre of the leg, sweeping it wide. A keeper who slides recklessly and hopes for the best will be sent off in due course.

## Chapter 29 — The Header

On a chipped ball over the back line, the keeper sometimes must clear with the head. This is a technique outfield defenders have spent their careers training and goalkeepers have not, which means most goalkeepers are bad at it. They should not be.

The technique is the same as a defensive header by any other player. The keeper attacks the ball, jumping off one foot if running, both feet if stationary, and contacts the ball with the forehead — the bony plate above the eyebrows — driving it upward and outward. The neck is locked at the moment of contact; a soft neck produces an uncontrolled deflection.

The aim is to clear the danger, not to direct a precise pass. A header that clears the eighteen-yard line and goes to the touchline is a successful sweeping action. A header that stays in the danger zone, even if it is a controlled pass, is a failure.

## Chapter 30 — Reading the Press

The modern keeper's sweeping is not just defensive — it is also part of the team's pressing structure. When the team presses high, the keeper supports the press by being available as a passing outlet for defenders, by sweeping behind them to handle balls played over the top, and by communicating the press's organisation.

This is a tactical contribution as much as a technical one. The keeper sees the entire pressing structure from behind it, knows which players are committing forward and which are holding, and is responsible for warning when the structure breaks down. A keeper who does not communicate during the press is leaving their team blind to the situation directly in front of them.

## Chapter 31 — Recovery After a Sweep

A sweeping action that has succeeded — the ball is cleared, the danger is gone — is followed by a recovery jog back toward the goal as the team reorganises. A sweeping action that has failed — the keeper has missed the ball or been beaten to it — is followed by a sprint back to the goal line and an attempt to defend the second-phase shot from a deep position.

In either case, the keeper does not stop watching the play. The danger may have passed, but the next phase of attack may begin within seconds, and a keeper who has switched off after a successful clearance will be caught flat-footed when a quick attack develops on the rebound.

# Part VIII — Penalty Saving

*The penalty kick is a duel of single attempts. The taker has every advantage in the laws of the game — twelve yards, no defender, a stationary ball, a target three times wider than they need it to be. The keeper has only one advantage: the freedom to act first, before the kick has been taken, on whatever information they can extract from the taker's body. This part describes how to extract that information, how to act on it, and how to maintain the psychological composure that penalty saving requires.*

## Chapter 32 — Stance and Set on the Line

The keeper's starting stance is on the line — modern laws require at least one foot on the goal line, with no part of the foot ahead of the line, at the moment the ball is struck. The stance is similar to a standard set position with two adjustments. The feet are slightly wider than shoulder-width to maximise lateral drive. The hands are held lower, at hip height rather than waist, because the most likely save is at low or mid height and the hands have less far to travel from this position.

The body is square to the ball. The eyes are on the taker, not the ball; this seems counterintuitive but is correct. The ball is stationary and tells the keeper nothing. The taker's body is moving, and tells the keeper everything.

## Chapter 33 — Reading the Cues

The penalty kick has a small number of reliable cues. A keeper who has trained to read them is dramatically more successful than one who guesses.

### 33.1 — The Run-Up Angle

The angle from which the taker approaches the ball provides a weak but real signal. A run-up at a sharp angle to the ball — typically thirty to forty-five degrees — favours a shot to the natural side, which is the side opposite to the kicking foot. A run-up that is more direct — under twenty degrees — keeps both sides available. A run-up that is unusually direct, almost straight on, often indicates a planned chip down the middle.

This signal is weak because skilled takers vary their run-up angles deliberately. But the base rate still holds: a taker with a sharp-angled approach has slightly tilted the probabilities toward their natural side, and a keeper who is aware of this tilt can incorporate it into their final decision.

### 33.2 — The Plant Foot

As described in Chapter 5, the plant foot is the strongest single predictor of shot direction. On a penalty, the plant foot tells the keeper which side the ball is going almost without exception. The plant happens approximately a third of a second before contact — long enough for the keeper to begin moving in the right direction, short enough that the keeper cannot wait too long after this cue to act.

### 33.3 — The Body Lean

As described in Chapter 5, the body lean predicts the height of the shot. On a penalty, the lean is also a partial predictor of placement: a taker leaning back tends to favour the upper portion of the goal, where their natural mechanics are best suited; a taker over the ball tends to favour low driven shots.

### 33.4 — The Eyes

Some takers look at where they intend to shoot. Most do not, and indeed many deliberately look away from their target. The keeper should not rely on eye-direction as a predictor; it is too easily faked. But the keeper can sometimes catch a moment of pre-kick eye-flick toward the target, especially in younger or less experienced takers. This is a bonus signal, not a primary one.

## Chapter 34 — The Static Dive

From the on-line set position, the keeper has two basic options: dive in the moment the plant foot lands, or take a small step in the predicted direction before diving. The static dive is the first option.

The keeper reads the plant foot and drives off the same leg as the predicted direction — left leg drive for a dive to the left, right leg drive for a dive to the right. The body extends laterally, with the timing matched so that the keeper is at full extension at the moment the ball is roughly halfway between the spot and the goal. The catch is rarely possible at full pace; the realistic target is a parry away from goal or, on weaker shots, a clean catch.

The static dive is the simpler of the two techniques and is what most goalkeepers default to. It is reliable but it does not maximise reach; the keeper has not added a step's worth of lateral momentum before launching.

## Chapter 35 — The Step Dive

The step dive — sometimes called the step-and-launch or the cha-cha by older coaches — is the technique of taking a single small step in the predicted direction before driving into the dive. The step is taken in the moment after the plant-foot read but before the kicking foot strikes the ball.

The step is short — perhaps fifteen centimetres — and the keeper must land the step with the foot in contact with the line. (Modern penalty rules require at least one foot on the line at the moment of contact; the step must satisfy this constraint.) From the landing of the step, the keeper drives into a normal diving save, but with a metre or more of additional reach compared to the static dive.

The step dive is the elite technique and is what most professional keepers use. It demands precise timing — too early and the keeper has committed before the read is final; too late and the step is wasted because the ball has already been struck — and it demands trust in the read. A keeper who hesitates between the step and the dive will produce neither and will be beaten cleanly.

## Chapter 36 — The Centre Save

Approximately twenty-five per cent of penalties are struck down the middle of the goal. A keeper who dives to one side or the other on every penalty will, by the laws of arithmetic, miss this category entirely.

The centre save is the keeper holding their ground, lifting the body upward and slightly outward, and saving the ball with the legs, the chest, or — on a chip — the hands rising backward overhead. It is technically the easiest save in the penalty repertoire but psychologically the hardest, because the keeper must commit to standing still while a ball is struck toward them at velocity.

The cue for the centre save is a taker whose run-up is unusually direct, whose plant foot is square to the ball rather than angled, and whose body has not leaned back. These cues together point to a centred shot. A keeper who can read them and hold position will save penalties that no diver could reach.

## Chapter 37 — The Mind Game

The penalty is half technical and half psychological. The taker is under pressure; the keeper is under pressure of a different kind. Small actions before the kick can shift the balance.

The keeper who walks slowly to the line, takes a deliberate moment to clean the gloves, and stares at the taker without smiling has communicated something — not certainty, but composure. The keeper who fidgets, paces back and forth, or speaks too much has communicated nervousness. Takers, especially under fatigue and end-of-match pressure, are sensitive to these communications.

This is not gamesmanship in the cheap sense. It is professional preparation. The keeper has rehearsed their pre-penalty routine and executes it; the routine is calming for the keeper and unsettling for the taker. The same routine on every penalty produces the same internal state every time. Routines, like techniques, are trained.

# Part IX — Reflex and Reactive Saves

*Some shots cannot be read in advance. A deflection off a defender, a redirected ball off a teammate, a shot that swerves unpredictably in the last metre, a rebound off the keeper's own first save — these are situations where the standard reading techniques fail and the keeper must rely on what is loosely called reflex. Reflex, however, is not what it seems. It is not the absence of technique. It is technique compressed into the smallest possible time window.*

## Chapter 38 — The Anatomy of a Reflex Save

A so-called reflex save is performed in roughly two hundred milliseconds — the time between the keeper recognising the new trajectory of the ball and the contact at the saving point. Within those two hundred milliseconds, the keeper must process the new direction, select a technique, initiate the movement, and execute the save.

The processing happens unconsciously. A keeper who is consciously thinking about a save in this time window will fail; the conscious mind is too slow. Instead, the keeper relies on a library of pre-trained responses that fire automatically when certain visual patterns are recognised. Train the library, and the responses are available; neglect to train it, and the keeper will be in conscious thought during the moment they have no time for conscious thought.

This is what underlies the famous principle, attributed variously to Tim Howard and Edwin van der Sar: you do not save shots you have not trained for. The keeper who has not trained the reaction-deflection in their session will not make the deflection-save in the match, regardless of athletic ability.

## Chapter 39 — The Recovery Save

After a parry, the ball is in play and an attacker may be onto the rebound within a fraction of a second. The recovery save is the keeper's second action — getting up from the ground, regaining a set position, and saving the rebounded shot.

The technical content is mostly speed. The keeper has no time to organise the perfect technique; they must arrive at the ball with whatever shape they can manage. The standard sequence is: roll toward the ball, push up on the lower hand, drive the legs underneath, and arrive in a low set facing the threat. This is the recovery sequence described in Chapter 14.

The save itself is then often a block save — described in Chapter 26 — because the keeper has no time to dive. The body is square to the shot, the hands are wide, the legs are spread low. The keeper has settled for a high-percentage block rather than a low-percentage save attempt, and the choice is correct.

## Chapter 40 — The Reactive Save (Deflection)

When a shot is deflected by a defender or teammate in flight, the keeper has perhaps a tenth of a second to register the new trajectory and respond. The technique is whatever can be brought to bear in that time.

Most reactive saves are with the closer hand, palm presented to the new direction of the ball. The keeper does not have time to dive, to organise the body, to choose between catch and parry. They have time to put a hand on the ball, and the hand is whatever hand was already raised. The save is almost always a parry rather than a catch, because the keeper's hand position has not been organised for catching.

The training for this is extensive deflection drilling. A coach with a deflection mat, or a partner standing on the goal line who randomly redirects shots, can produce hundreds of these scenarios in a session. The keeper's library of automatic responses grows with every repetition. Over time, the keeper is no longer making a hundred-millisecond decision; they are executing a pre-built pattern.

## Chapter 41 — The Goal-Line Clearance

The goal-line clearance is the last-resort save: a ball has crossed the keeper and is heading into the goal, and the keeper must reach back to deflect it before it crosses the line. This is the most desperate save in the repertoire and the one with the lowest probability of success.

The technique is improvisational. The keeper twists, reaches, often falls; the contact is with whatever surface — back of the hand, foot, head, body — gets there first. There is no canonical technique, only the principle that any contact is better than none and any deflection is better than no deflection.

Drill it occasionally, in the same way one drills the starfish — not as a primary tool but as a contingency. A keeper who has touched the ball back from the line a few times in training will not be paralysed by the situation in a match.

## Chapter 42 — Sustaining Reflexes Through Fatigue

Reflexes degrade with fatigue. A keeper who is sharp in the first ten minutes of a match may be slow in the eighty-fifth, and a goal conceded late is exactly as costly as one conceded early. The training implication is that reactive drills should be done both fresh and tired. The keeper who has only trained reflexes in the first ten minutes of a session has not trained for the conditions in which they will need them most.

Plyometric and reactive drills at the end of a session, with the legs already heavy from the earlier work, are uncomfortable. They are also where the marginal training benefit is highest. A keeper who has saved a hundred deflections in their fresh state and another hundred in their fatigued state has built a more robust library than a keeper who has saved two hundred in the fresh state alone.

# Part X — Special Conditions

*All of the techniques in this encyclopedia have been described as if conditions were neutral: a true grass surface, no wind, no rain, no sun, no mud. Real matches are rarely played in such conditions. The keeper must be able to adjust every technique to the conditions of the day, and the adjustments are sometimes large.*

## Chapter 43 — Wet Conditions

A wet ball is a different ball. It is heavier, faster off the surface, and more prone to skidding. A wet glove is a different glove — the friction between glove and ball is reduced, sometimes dramatically depending on the rubber compound and the type of moisture.

### 43.1 — Adjustments to Catching

In wet conditions, catches that would be routine on a dry pitch become marginal. The contour grip is less secure because the ball can slide between the thumbs even when the hand position is correct. The keeper compensates by exaggerating the absorption phase — bringing the ball further into the body before securing — and by using two-barrier techniques wherever possible. The chest catch and the abdomen catch are preferred over the W-catch when the ball's trajectory permits it.

Punching and parrying both become more attractive options in wet weather, because they do not require a clean catch to succeed. The decision-making framework — described in Chapter 19 — shifts in favour of the safer outcome. A goalkeeper who tries to catch every cross in the rain will spill some of them; a goalkeeper who punches the marginal ones will not.

### 43.2 — Adjustments to Diving

Wet grass is faster than dry grass. A ball that would have rolled to the keeper on a dry pitch will skid past them on a wet one. The keeper's reaction time must shorten correspondingly; they must commit to the dive earlier, sometimes before the read would normally feel complete.

The landing on a wet pitch is, paradoxically, often softer than on a dry one — the surface gives more — but it is also less predictable. The keeper may slide further than expected on contact with the ground. This is why wet-pitch training matters: a keeper who has only trained on dry surfaces will be surprised by the slide and may lose control of the ball during the slide phase.

### 43.3 — The Long Barrier in the Wet

This is where the long barrier earns its place in the modern syllabus. On a wet pitch, the long barrier's three-barrier safety becomes more valuable because the probability of a fumble is higher. A keeper who would default to the standing scoop on a dry pitch should default to the long barrier on a wet one. The recovery cost is real but acceptable; the cost of a goal conceded from a fumbled scoop is greater.

## Chapter 44 — Wind

Wind affects flighted balls more than driven balls. A long high cross can be moved a metre or more by a strong wind during its flight; a low driven cross is barely affected.

The keeper reads the wind from the flags, the corner flags being the most useful indicator. They pre-adjust their position to compensate: stand a metre further back if the wind is blowing the ball into the goal; stand a metre further forward if the wind is blowing it away. These adjustments are continuous and must be re-evaluated every few minutes as wind direction shifts.

On set pieces, wind can change the flight of a corner significantly. An in-swinger that becomes more in-swinging because of a tailwind, or an out-swinger that becomes neutral because of a headwind, demands different positioning than the same delivery on a still day. The keeper communicates this to the defenders before the corner is taken — a windy day is one in which the pre-set-piece conversation matters more, not less.

## Chapter 45 — Sun

A keeper who has the sun directly behind the attacking team's shooting position is at a significant visual disadvantage. The standard adjustment is to wear a cap — the brim shading the eyes from direct sun — and to position slightly offset from the direct line of the ball, so that the sun is not directly over the attacker's shoulder.

More importantly, the keeper trains for visual occlusion. Drills in which a ball is briefly obscured by traffic or by the position of the sun teach the keeper to track based on partial information and to commit to a saving action even when the visual track has been interrupted.

## Chapter 46 — Pitch Quality and Bobbles

On uneven pitches — and many pitches at semi-professional level are uneven — the ball does not roll true. Bobbles are unpredictable; a routine ground ball may suddenly skip up at the keeper's chest, or dive to the keeper's feet.

The defensive technique is the long barrier, used by default rather than situationally. The three-barrier safety covers the keeper for most bobbles. The standing scoop, with its single barrier, is exposed.

On a true pitch, the standing scoop is fine. The decision rule is: scoop on truly true surfaces; long-barrier on anything else. A keeper who has not assessed the pitch in the warm-up has not done their preparation. Five minutes of rolling balls along the pitch in different areas will reveal where the bobbles are likely and where the surface is true.

# Appendix A — Glossary

Technical terms in alphabetical order, with the cross-reference to the chapter where each is described in detail. Where a term has multiple recognised names across European coaching traditions, all names are listed.

### Above-head catch

Catching technique used when the ball arrives above the keeper's standing height. Performed with one-foot take-off, opposite knee driven up, and contour grip formed before the catch. See Chapter 8.

### Abdomen catch

Catching technique for a ball arriving at stomach height with the body in line. Hands meet the ball in front of the abdomen; the abdomen contracts to absorb. See Chapter 7.

### Backward dive

Diving save toward a ball that has been struck or deflected over the keeper's head. Performed with a twist in flight to land sideways rather than flat on the back. See Chapter 13.

### Block save

Close-range saving technique used when there is no time to dive or organise a K-barrier. The keeper presents a wide silhouette with knees and arms spread. Associated with David de Gea. See Chapter 26.

### Boxing

Two-fisted punch with knuckles together, used to clear a high ball with maximum force. Also called the two-fist punch. See Chapter 17.

### Caduta

Italian term for the collapse save. See Chapter 13.

### Chest catch

Catch in which the ball is received against the chest with the body in line. The most secure handling outcome in the goalkeeper's repertoire. See Chapter 7.

### Collapse save

The lowest member of the dive family. The keeper folds the legs and falls sideways onto the ball without flying. Italian: caduta; Dutch: valbeweging. See Chapter 13.

### Contour grip

The W-shaped hand position used for catching balls at chest height or above. Thumbs touch behind the ball; fingers spread to form a W. See Chapter 4.

### Cross-step

Lateral footwork pattern in which the trail leg crosses in front of the lead leg. Used for covering distance; sacrifices body squareness. See Chapter 2.

### Cup

Hand shape for catching at head height when there is no time to form a full contour grip. Palms face each other directly; thumbs alongside the index fingers. See Chapter 4.

### Deflection (wrist)

Last-resort redirection using the back of the hand or wrist. The keeper has minimal control over the rebound direction. See Chapter 11.

### Drop-step

Backward-diagonal footwork pattern. The lead leg pivots open and steps backward at forty-five degrees; the trail leg follows in a crossing stride. The fundamental movement for a high-line keeper. See Chapter 2.

### Forward parry

Parry directed forward into open space rather than wide. Used when wide redirection is not possible. See Chapter 9.

### High dive (power save)

Dive toward the upper corner of the goal. Combines maximum lateral and vertical drive to produce a horizontal flight in the air. See Chapter 13.

### Hollow chest / hollow stomach

Coaching cue for the inward draw of the chest or abdomen at the moment of catching. Creates a yielding surface that absorbs the ball. See Chapters 4 and 7.

### In-swinger

Cross that curves toward the goal as it travels. Produced by a right-footed crosser delivering from the left wing, or vice versa. See Chapter 15.

### K-barrier (Spanish K)

One-against-one defensive shape originating in futsal. The keeper stays on their feet with one knee dropped low, presenting a barrier in the shape of the letter K. The signature modern 1v1 technique. See Chapter 22.

### Loaded

The body shape immediately after the shot has been struck, when the legs have begun to drive but the hands have not yet committed to a direction. One of the five shapes of a save. See Chapter 3.

### Long barrier

Ground-ball catching technique with one knee dropped to form a long barrier with the lower leg. Provides three-barrier safety. The default ground-ball technique on wet pitches. See Chapters 6 and 23.

### Low dive

Diving save at knee or ankle height beyond an arm's reach. The workhorse dive of professional goalkeeping. See Chapter 13.

### Out-swinger

Cross that curves away from the goal as it travels. Produced by a right-footed crosser delivering from the right wing, or vice versa. See Chapter 15.

### Parry (strong)

Controlled redirection of a ball using the palm and rigid wrist. The keeper drives the ball away from danger with a deliberate destination. See Chapter 9.

### Plant foot

The non-kicking foot of a shooter. The strongest single predictor of shot direction. See Chapter 5.

### Power step

Forty-five-degree drive with the lead leg, the launching mechanism for diving saves above ankle height. See Chapter 2.

### Recovery save

The keeper's second saving action after a parry has produced a rebound. The technique is whatever shape the keeper can produce in the available time, often a block save. See Chapter 39.

### Scoop (cradle)

Hand shape for catching ground balls. Palms forward, fingers down, little fingers nearly touching. See Chapter 4.

### Set position

The body shape adopted in the moment immediately before a shot. Feet shoulder-width, weight forward over the toes, hands at waist, eyes on the ball. The foundation of every save. See Chapter 1.

### Set-set

The rhythmic version of the set position used during a one-against-one approach. The keeper sets, advances, and sets again in rhythm with the attacker's touches. See Chapters 1 and 21.

### Side-step (shuffle)

Lateral footwork pattern with feet that never cross. The default lateral movement. See Chapter 2.

### Smother

Forward-diagonal dive onto a loose ball at the attacker's feet, used when the attacker has taken a heavy touch. The keeper must reach the ball before the boot. See Chapter 24.

### Soft hands

The principle of catching by absorption rather than rigid stopping. Achieved through a controlled give in the elbows and wrists at the moment of contact. Not weak hands. See Chapter 4.

### Starfish (star jump, spread)

Point-blank emergency technique in which the keeper jumps and spreads all four limbs to occupy the maximum silhouette. Associated with Peter Schmeichel. The last technique, not the first. See Chapter 25.

### Step dive

Penalty-saving technique in which the keeper takes a single step in the predicted direction before launching into the dive. Adds a metre or more of reach over the static dive. See Chapter 35.

### Sweeper-keeper

A goalkeeper whose resting position is high — typically on or near the eighteen-yard line — and who participates actively in defensive sweeping behind a high line. See Part VII.

### Tip-over

Redirection of a rising ball over the crossbar, typically with both hands or with the strong-side hand. See Chapter 10.

### Through ball

Pass played behind the defensive line for an attacker to run onto. The action that most often triggers a sweeper-keeper response. See Chapter 27.

### Valbeweging

Dutch term for the collapse movement. See Chapter 13.

### W-catch

Common English term for the contour grip. See above.

### Weak-side tip

Single-hand tip with the trail hand — the hand opposite to the side of the dive. Mechanically harder than the strong-side tip but often the only available option. See Chapter 10.

# Appendix C — Common Errors and Corrections

The errors a coach will see most often, with the corrections that work in practice. These are the persistent errors — the ones that recur even in well-trained goalkeepers when fatigue, pressure, or inattention return them to bad habits. They are not the errors of a beginner learning a technique for the first time; those have a different list.

### Heel contact in the set position

Symptom: keeper appears set on video but is half a metre slow on every dive. Visible on a side-angle camera as weight sitting back on the heels.

Correction: drill the set with heels lifted off the ground by a paper's thickness. Have the keeper hold this position for ten seconds at a time. Re-introduce flat feet only after the keeper is comfortable in the toes-on position.

### Crossing the feet under pressure

Symptom: keeper takes a cross-step where a side-step was correct, loses body squareness in the moment of the shot, and is beaten down the centre.

Correction: drill repetitions of small-distance lateral movements with explicit instruction not to cross. Use a coach's call — shuffle or cross — to reinforce the choice. Most keepers cross because they want to cover ground faster; remove the pressure and they will side-step correctly.

### Hands ahead of the body in the set

Symptom: keeper's hands are at chest height, arms outstretched, before the shot is taken. The reach for the catch is too long; shock absorption is gone.

Correction: drill the set with hands held visibly at waist height. Use a tactile cue — have the keeper feel the hands brush the front of the shorts — to reinforce the position. Hands rise from the waist as the shot is taken, not before.

### Diving across the body

Symptom: on a low dive, the keeper's body passes over the ball rather than to it. The hands miss the ball or arrive late.

Correction: re-establish the forty-five-degree power step. Use a cone or chalk line on the ground to mark the angle. The lead foot must land closer to the ball's path than to the side of the body.

### Catching with elbows locked

Symptom: the ball spills on contact even when the hand position is correct. Visible on slow-motion video as the lack of any flex in the arms during the catch.

Correction: drill catches with explicit instruction to bend the elbows on contact. Use a verbal cue at the moment of the catch — soft, or absorb. Watch the elbows in video review until the bend is consistent.

### Late or missing communication on crosses

Symptom: defenders and goalkeeper end up going for the same ball, or the ball lands in a no-man's-land between them.

Correction: drill cross scenarios with the explicit requirement to call before the ball reaches the box. A keeper who does not call gives up the rep. Build the calling habit into the muscle memory of the cross-handling decision.

### Sliding in 1v1 when the K-barrier was correct

Symptom: keeper goes to ground, the attacker dribbles around them, the goal is open.

Correction: drill 1v1 scenarios with the explicit instruction to stay on the feet unless the touch is heavy enough to smother. The default is the K-barrier; the slide is the exception. Reinforce with video review of the slide-versus-K decision.

### Fading reflexes in the second half

Symptom: keeper sharp in the first ten minutes, slow in the eighty-fifth. Goals conceded late in matches at a higher rate than early.

Correction: end every training session with reactive drills under fatigue. Build the keeper's library of automatic responses in the tired state, not just the fresh state. The match conditions for late saves are tired conditions; train accordingly.

# Appendix D — A Closing Word

This encyclopedia describes the techniques. It does not describe the work.

The work is what happens between Tuesday and Friday, when no one is watching, in the cold and the rain and the dark. It is the hundredth repetition of a set position, the thousandth repetition of a contour grip, the ten-thousandth repetition of a power step. It is the willingness to be coached, to accept correction, to undo a habit that took five years to build because it was the wrong habit.

A goalkeeper who has read this book and not done the work knows more than they did before, but they will save no more shots. A goalkeeper who has done the work without ever opening this book may save many shots, but they will save them inefficiently — by accident as often as by design — and they will struggle to teach the next generation what they themselves cannot put into words.

The encyclopedia, in other words, is for both. The reading explains what the work is for. The work makes the reading worth something.

To the goalkeeper coaches who are using this book on a Friday evening before a Saturday match: I hope you find what you came for. Take what is useful. Argue with what is not. Add to it from your own experience, because the book is not finished — no book on goalkeeping ever can be — and the next generation will need a better version than this one.

To the goalkeepers themselves: the position you have chosen is the most demanding on the pitch. It is also the most rewarding, because the saves you make win matches that no one else could have won. Be patient with the technique. Be patient with yourself. The work is long, and it is also worth it.
