A word on the voice of this document

The position, more readable
than it looks.

Coaches don't call it a "save attempt"; they call it a save. They don't say the keeper "blocked the goal"; they say he made it small. The vocabulary of this document is the vocabulary of the dashboard.

Goalkeeping has a culture and a language. We use the coaches' vocabulary throughout because that's what the dashboard speaks back to them. You'll pick it up. If you have never played football, you can still label well. The position is more readable than it looks once you know what to watch.

People who watch football casually think a goalkeeper does one thing — stops shots. That's roughly 5% of the job in modern football. The actual job has four parts: shot stopping, crossing & aerial, 1v1s and through balls, and distribution & build-up. You will be labeling all four.

The vocabulary for each is in the chapters that follow. Read them in order. Don't skim. The cost of a mis-labeled training example is paid by every coach who reads the model's output downstream.

From the field

"Honest 'I couldn't tell' beats a confident guess every time. The model will eventually train on what you label — wrong labels teach wrong lessons."

§ 03 · The save action

Watch the hands. Watch the ball
after contact.

When there was a save event, classify the keeper's action by what their hands and body did at the moment of contact. Do not infer from the trajectory alone.

01Catch

Both hands close around the ball, fingers form a "W" or basket behind the ball. Keeper retains the ball after contact — it stays in their possession.

If you do not see clear two-handed retention, it is not a Catch.

02Block

Ball stops on the keeper's body or one hand without secure retention. Often produces a rebound.

The keeper does NOT have control of the ball after contact; it bounces away or is collected on the second action.

03Parry

Keeper's hand(s) intentionally redirect the ball away from danger — wide, over the bar, to ground in front. The redirect is purposeful.

You can see the wrist or palm push the ball in a chosen direction. A strong parry pushes hard and far; a weak parry leaves a dangerous rebound.

04Deflect

Ball glances off the keeper without an intentional redirect. Often a fingertip touch on a shot heading just over or wide.

If the keeper barely got a hand on it and the ball mostly continues its original line, it is a Deflect.

A parry has wrist action. A deflect has no wrist action. The difference between a learned skill and an accident — and the difference coaches care about.

§ 04 · Cheat sheet

Save action cheat sheet.

Pin this near your screen during the first matches. After ten labelings you will not need it.

Table 04.1 · GK action by recognition cue
If you see… The label is Common pitfall
Two-handed W behind the ball, keeper holds Catch Fumbled cleanly = Block, not Catch
Ball stops on body or one hand, rebound likely Block If hands open and ball held = Catch
Hand pushes ball wide / over / to ground with intent Parry Watch the wrist — passive hand = Deflect
Ball glances off passive hand, continues path Deflect Confused with Parry — see § 03.3
Clenched fist(s) strike the ball clear Punch Open palms = Block, not Punch
Keeper goes to ground before shot, wraps ball Smother Usually a 1v1 — note in observations
Keeper should have saved but didn't Missed Don't use for unstoppable strikes
Ball in the net Goal Logged in Goals sheet separately
Couldn't tell, or keeper not visible Unclear Use freely — honest beats confident

When in doubt between two labels, mark Unclear. Reviewers prefer 30% Unclear to 30% wrong — and one of the few rules with no exceptions.

§ 05 · Pitch geometry

The language coaches use for space.

Most labeling errors trace to not knowing where on the pitch a thing happened. Left and right of shot origin is from the attacker's perspective. Left and right of goal placement flips to the goalkeeper's perspective.
