§ 01 · Introduction

A word on the voice of this document.

The primer teaches you to see what a goalkeeper coach sees. Same vocabulary, same definitions, the analyzer uses.

Goalkeeping has a culture and a language. Coaches don't call it a "save attempt"; they call it a save. They don't say the keeper "blocked the goal"; they say he made it small. We use the coaches' vocabulary throughout because that's what the dashboard speaks back to them. You'll pick it up.

If you have never played football, you can still label well. The position is more readable than it looks once you know what to watch.

What a goalkeeper actually does

People who watch football casually think a goalkeeper does one thing — stops shots. That's roughly 5% of the job in modern football. The actual job has four parts:

Shot stopping. Reaction to a ball struck toward goal. Subdivided by where the shot came from, how it arrived (foot, head, deflection), and what the keeper did to deal with it (catch, parry, block, punch, deflect, beat, conceded).

Crossing & aerial. Anything in the air into the box that the keeper claims, punches, or chooses to leave. A keeper's command of the area is judged by how decisively they take or stay on crosses.

1v1s and through balls. Situations where an attacker is bearing down on goal in space. Different toolkit — smother, K-barrier, spread save, narrowing the angle. Coaches study these separately.

Distribution & build-up. What the keeper does after they have the ball. In modern football this is half of the job. A keeper who can't play out of the back has a short ceiling.

Why this matters for labeling
Each of these four areas has its own sheet in the labeling workbook: Saves for shot stopping events with the keeper's action and body distance Crosses for aerial-claim decisions and starting position 1v1s for the big moments where the rubric doesn't fit Distribution for every release, classified by motion not by distance

§ 03 · Shot stopping

The save action — what the keeper actually did.

Classify by what the hands and body did at the moment of contact. Watch the hands. Watch the ball after contact. Do not infer from the trajectory alone.

Recognition cues

Catch

Both hands close around the ball, fingers form a "W" or basket behind the ball. Keeper retains the ball after contact — it stays in their possession. If you do not see clear two-handed retention, it is not a Catch.

Block

Ball stops on the keeper's body or one hand without secure retention. Often produces a rebound. The keeper does NOT have control after contact; the ball bounces away or is collected on the second action.

Parry

Keeper's hand(s) intentionally redirect the ball away from danger — wide, over the bar, to ground in front. The redirect is purposeful: you can see the wrist or palm push the ball in a chosen direction.

Deflect

Ball glances off the keeper without an intentional redirect. Often a fingertip touch on a shot heading just over or wide. If the ball mostly continues its original line, it is a Deflect — not a Parry.

Punch

Clenched fist or two-fisted strike. Most common on crosses but can occur on shots when the keeper can't handle the ball cleanly. The hands are formed into a fist, not open palms.

Missed

The keeper should have made the save but didn't. Poor positioning, slow reaction, mishandle. Use this when the keeper was clearly at fault on a goal or near-miss.

Parry vs Deflect · The disagreement
Watch the wrist. A parry has wrist action — the palm pushes the ball. A deflect has no wrist action — the ball glances off a passive hand.

A strong parry that lands safely is a coach's dream. A deflect that ends up over the bar was lucky. Coaches care about the difference because it's the difference between a learned skill and an accident.

When in doubt, mark unclear.
