Introduction
§ 01 · Introduction

A word on the voice of this document.

This primer is written from the perspective of someone who has played the goalkeeping position professionally and coached it for four decades. The vocabulary here is exact — the same words, with the same definitions, the analyzer uses.

Goalkeeping has a culture and a language. Coaches don't call it a "save attempt"; they call it a save. They don't say the keeper "blocked the goal"; they say he made it small. We use the coaches' vocabulary throughout because that's what the dashboard speaks back to them. You'll pick it up.

If you have never played football, you can still label well. The position is more readable than it looks once you know what to watch.

People who watch football casually think a goalkeeper does one thing — stops shots. That's roughly 5% of the job in modern football. From § 02 — What the keeper actually does

What you'll find in this document

Four chapters, in order:

  • Shot stopping — the headline. Reaction to a ball struck toward goal. Subdivided by where the shot came from, how it arrived, and what the keeper did to deal with it.
  • Crossing & aerial — anything in the air into the box that the keeper claims, punches, or chooses to leave.
  • 1v1s and through balls — different toolkit, different decision tree. Coaches study these separately.
  • Distribution & build-up — what the keeper does after they have the ball. Half of the modern job.

You will be labeling all four. The vocabulary for each is in the next chapter.

Shot stopping
§ 03 · Shot stopping

The save action — what the keeper actually did.

When there was a save event, classify the keeper's action by what their hands and body did at the moment of contact. Watch the hands. Watch the ball after contact. Don't infer from the trajectory alone.

Recognition cues

Distinguishing between save types requires watching the goalkeeper's hands, body, and the ball's behaviour at the moment of contact. Use these cues; do not guess.

01Catch

Both hands close around the ball, fingers form a “W” or basket behind the ball, keeper retains the ball without dropping it. Ball stays in the keeper's possession after contact. If you do not see clear two-handed retention, it is not a Catch.

02Block

Ball stops on the keeper's body or one hand without secure retention. Often produces a rebound. The keeper does NOT have control of the ball after contact; it bounces away or is collected on the second action.

03Parry

Keeper's hand(s) intentionally redirect the ball away from danger (wide, over the bar, to ground in front). The redirect is purposeful: you can see the wrist or palm push the ball in a chosen direction. A strong parry pushes hard and far away from the goal; a weak parry leaves a rebound in dangerous territory.

A parry has wrist action — the palm pushes the ball in a direction. A deflect has no wrist action — the ball glances off a passive hand. Coaches care about the difference because it's the difference between a learned skill and an accident. Internal · The #1 disagreement in calibration

04Deflect

Ball glances off the keeper without an intentional redirect. Often a fingertip touch on a shot heading just over or wide. Lower-confidence contact than a parry. If the keeper barely got a hand on it and the ball continues mostly along its original line, it is a Deflect.

Save action cheat sheet.

Quick lookup. Pin this near your screen during the first matches. After ten labelings you will not need it.

Table 04.1 · GK action by recognition cue
If you see… The label is Common pitfall
Two-handed W behind the ball, keeper holds Catch Fumbled cleanly = Block, not Catch
Ball stops on body or one hand, rebound likely Block If hands open and ball held = Catch
Hand pushes ball wide / over / to ground with intent Parry Watch the wrist — passive hand = Deflect
Ball glances off passive hand, mostly continues path Deflect Confused with Parry — see § 03.3
Clenched fist(s) strike the ball clear Punch Open palms = Block, not Punch
Keeper goes to ground BEFORE the shot, body wraps the ball Smother Usually a 1v1 — note in observations
Keeper should have saved but didn't — poor position, mishandle Missed Don't use for unstoppable strikes
Ball in the net Goal Logged in Goals sheet separately

When in doubt between two labels, mark Unclear. Reviewers prefer 30% Unclear to 30% wrong.
